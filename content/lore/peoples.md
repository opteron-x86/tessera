# Tessera — Peoples

*A companion document to the world reference. This file holds the elder-race theories, current peoples, made peoples, riftborn, vampires, and were-creatures formerly summarized in `world.md`.*

---

## The Elder Races (Urath)

Scholars and oral tradition across multiple cultures reference an ancient people who predated the current division of races. The academic term, reconstructed from linguistic fragments at Tindaire, is **Elathi** — meaning roughly "the first people." In older oral traditions, particularly among the Dyrathi, the name **Urath** survives. What can be recovered about them is fragmentary and contested.

The Kynathi claim direct divine descent from the Urath, positioning their own people as the truest inheritors of Tessera's original civilized lineage. In Kynathi theology, the Urath were not mortal in the way current peoples are mortal — they were the first divine inhabitants of the world, and the Kynathi's magical gifts are evidence of this inheritance. This claim is not accepted by all peoples.

What is more broadly accepted is that the Kynathi, Alnathi, Myrathi, and Dyrathi share some ancient common ancestry, reflected in the *-athi* suffix that appears across all their names. Whether this reflects a literal shared lineage or is a linguistic artifact of long cohabitation is debated. The Dyrathi are divided on it: some clans accept the elder-races concept and embrace kinship with the elven peoples; others reject the connection entirely; a few hold their own divergent tradition in which the Urath were a subterranean people whose surface descendants eventually became the elven races, not the other way around. The extinct Vorathi — the flesh-shapers credited with the making of the orcish and goblinoid peoples — complicate the question further: if they were a true elven branch, then the made peoples stand in some relation to the *-athi* family that no living elven tradition is eager to define.

## Races

### Kynathi (High Elves)
Before the Spellbreak the Kynathi were the continental power: the **Kynathi Hegemony**, a civilization of extraordinary craft, learning, and ambition whose reach — direct, or through client peoples — covered much of Merata. Illuvinel, Arhebel, and Isrannaes are its bones. The catastrophe gutted it within a generation: the framework its high magic depended upon cracked, its god fell silent, and its client peoples — humans chief among them — went their own ways. What remains is the **Natari Dominion** (see [Geopolitics](geopolitics.md)): diminished, not weak. Long-lived and slow to breed — a Kynathi may see nine centuries — they have never recovered their numbers, and never will on any timescale that matters to their rivals.

The length of Kynathi life gives the present age its particular cruelty: the eldest among them personally remember the world before. They heard Aevorn answer prayer; they walked Illuvinel when its waterfalls ran. These witnesses are dying now, one by one — and in a world where memory is the substance of power, Natari understands precisely what it is losing. The Dominion is not a museum. It is a hospice of witnesses, and its edicts are enforced by people who remember why they were written. What the witnesses remember is not innocent: the golden age they carry is gilded — by grief, by pride, and by the ordinary way the old burnish their youth — and some of them know it, and gild it anyway.

They venerate the Avara, a pantheon of ancestor-gods, and believe themselves descended from the Urath, the divine first people of Tessera. Kynathi theology frames the Spellbreak as a wound to the divine order — and the silence of their chief god, Aevorn, as its deepest consequence.

Kynathi orthodoxy remembers the ancestors of the Alnathi as the **Anchorite heresy**: mystics who claimed a sight past the weave's edge, preached that creation itself must end, and were cast out for it in the age when the Avara still answered. That the catastrophe the heretics prophesied then arrived is a fact Kynathi theology has never comfortably metabolized. Orthodoxy resolves it the only way it can: the "foresight" was a Daevic whisper, and the whisperers helped make it true.

Some Kynathi view the Alnathi as aberrant, degenerate, or traitors to the elven heritage — from a Kynathi theological standpoint, Daeva worship is not merely heresy but a choice to bind oneself to dead things rather than seek the divine return. Formal Kynathi demonology — the **Doctrine of the Severed** — inverts the Alnathi approach entirely: it holds the Daeva to be dead limbs cut from creation, without true hierarchy or individuality, and teaches that every name fixed to them feeds them. The doctrine forbids naming, cataloguing, or studying the Daeva; at Natari it has the force of law, and the burden of forbidden knowledge is carried by a small sequestered order, the Wardens of the Unspoken.

### Alnathi (Dark Elves)
The Alnathi and the Kynathi are one people twice divided — first by heresy, then by catastrophe — and the schism was about the Daeva from the beginning.

Millennia before the Spellbreak, in the age when the Avara still answered, a current arose within elven orthodoxy: mystics of Luneth's and Morindel's cults — the hidden and the threshold — who turned their contemplation past the weave's edge and attained what their descendants call the **Grave-Sight**: a direct apprehension of the Graveland and of what it is. What they saw was a shore of dead creations — failed worlds and fallen makers — and they drew the inference orthodoxy could not tolerate: that this happens every time. Creation is not permanent; worlds are made, run their course, and end; the Graveland is not an aberration but a destination; and the Avara's promise of the return is a promise made inside a system that itself ends. Whether the seers were expelled or walked out of a temple that had become a cage is remembered differently on each side. That they went willingly, in the end, is disputed by no one. Tradition preserves no founder's name; the Alnathi hold that the first seer gave the name away, and do not say to what.

From the Grave-Sight the movement's schools diverged, and both survive as substrate in Alnathi tradition. The **Harbored** taught the Daeva-bond as refuge: a soul bound to the Graveland persists as the Daeva persist, and will not dissolve with creation when the end comes. The **Moorbound** taught something more radical — that souls bound to the Daeva anchor those powers to the living world, and in anchoring them, anchor the world itself: a mooring against the cycle's turning, prolonging creation in defiance of its makers' design. On the Moorbound reading, Daeva practice is not self-damnation but sacrifice — the practitioner spends their own return to keep the world alive — and the Avara's ancient hostility to the practice was never protection, but enforcement of a demolition schedule. No living Alnathi institution asserts either doctrine plainly. They persist beneath the surface: in funerary practice, in the Vigil's charge at Nerebyasca, in the deliberateness with which the Alnathi engage powers that other peoples flee. Outsiders call the movement, and the people it became, the **Anchorite heresy**; the name is Natari's, and the Alnathi long ago stopped objecting to it.

The exiles settled the continental interior — the region that became the Blightlands — and were at or near the center of the Spellbreak. Within a few generations of the catastrophe the transformation set in: skin turned gray, blue-tinged, deep purple. The cause is genuinely undecidable. Spellbreak exposure and generations of deliberate Daevic practice were operating on the same population at the same moment. The Kynathi accusation — that the change is Syrath's mark, the wage of the old heresy made flesh — is plausible; so is the Alnathi mainstream position that it is a Spellbreak scar, kin to the Sahridi sands. A few Alnathi mystics embrace the Syrath reading as an honor rather than a slander: what one becomes is what one always was. The truth is unrecoverable — and the Edict of Unnaming makes it doctrinally illegal for Natari ever to substantiate its own accusation.

Daeva worship is common among the Alnathi and is not considered evil in traditional Alnathi culture; it is approached with caution and deliberate practice, formalized over the post-Spellbreak centuries from what had been generations of forbidden discipline. Their favored Daeva — Leraye, Nymeris, Chayliel, Syrath — reflect a culture that values precision, adaptability, the capacity for self-transformation, and navigating the edges of power. The Daeva they avoid — Mahadron, Selveth, Nuvaal, Razael, Vaurun — are those that threaten or erase the self, rather than sharpen it. The Alnathi are not reckless in what they engage with; they are deliberate. The death-priests of Nerebyasca — the order known as **the Vigil** — compiled and keep the Nerebine Codex, the foundational mortal chart of the Graveland; the order predates the Codex by an unknown span, and may be as old as the exile itself.

The Alnathi survived the Spellbreak and its aftermath but tend not to expand territorially or assert dominance. They often find it difficult to integrate into societies where Kynathi-influenced prejudice is common. Some Alnathi clans have developed their own forms of racial superiority in response. Their homeland sits in rugged interior terrain adjacent to the Blightlands — territory other peoples cannot hold, and the only country the catastrophe left that is indisputably theirs.

For all the mutual hatred, the two elven peoples remain one temperament pointed in opposite directions: long-lived, deliberate, tradition-bound, organized around their dead, each maintaining a sequestered order that carries dangerous knowledge — the Wardens who refuse to write, the Vigil who control the writing. The Kynathi answered the Spellbreak with discipline aimed at restoring what was; the Alnathi with discipline aimed at mastering what is. Scholars at Tindaire note the symmetry constantly. Both peoples find the observation intolerable.

### Myrathi (Sea Elves)
The most visually and culturally distinct of the elven peoples. The Myrathi occupy the Shattered Isles in the Orathian Sea, and their impossibly fast warships are feared by all sailors — a Myrathi sail on the horizon is read as a death omen. The fear is earned but the reading is wrong, because there is no pattern for outsiders to read.

Myrathi culture turns on **the encounter**. Any meeting — a foreign vessel, a whale-run, a storm-wrack, a stranger cast ashore — must be acted upon as circumstance dictates, and circumstance is read in the moment by the ship's **tidecaller**: the winds, the water, the taste of salt and brine, the position of the stars or sun. The practice is called **the Reading**, and its verdict is absolute. The Myrathi do not raid, kill, pillage, or take captives for sport, plunder, or even survival; they do what the sea dictated. This is why no outsider ever learns why a Myrathi vessel killed all but one of a crew and carried him away, or disabled a ship and left it drifting, or drew alongside, did nothing, and departed. Someone taken by a Reading was not enslaved but *named by the sea* — some are returned years later, changed; some become Myrathi; some are delivered to a place and left there, for reasons no one, possibly including the Myrathi, will ever explain.

Whether something truly answers the Reading — the sea itself, something beneath it, something the Myrathi would not call a Daeva, or nothing at all — is unknown to outsiders and perhaps contested among the Myrathi themselves. Sailors of every nation keep countermeasures — offerings thrown overboard, silence on deck, hull markings — none of which verifiably work, which is worse than if they did. Seradon's admiralty maintains a corpus of encounter accounts; generations of study have found that the only variable predicting outcomes is conditions at the moment of contact, which is exactly what the Myrathi have been saying all along.

### Dyrathi (Dwarves)
The Dyrathi are ancestrally linked to the other *-athi* races through the ancient Urath lineage. Unlike the Kynathi and Giants, they are not in obvious decline — if anything, the opposite: sheltered underground from a substantially surface catastrophe, the Dyrathi came through the Spellbreak the least damaged of the great peoples, and seven centuries of careful solvency have made their holds the quiet creditors and toll-keepers of everyone else's recovery. They maintain substantial mountain holds, deep mines, and underground cities along the mountain range forming the northern edge of Merata's human heartland. Their holds control the key passes between the human kingdoms to the south and the wilder north — a geographic leverage that has given them a pragmatic importance that sheer population numbers do not reflect.

Some Dyrathi holdings are integrated into Meratan geopolitics through trade and treaty; others are entirely self-contained, hostile to outsiders, and indifferent to whatever happens on the surface. Fully integrated Dyrathi living among human or other cultures are not uncommon. The Dyrathi are renowned craftspeople — their forges produce the finest weapons and armor in Merata.

### Humans
Diverse, adaptable, and the continent's plurality: the most numerous people of Merata, and the fastest to recover from the catastrophe — but masters of nothing. Before the Spellbreak most human polities existed in the Kynathi Hegemony's shadow as clients, tributaries, and auxiliaries — but they had not begun that way. There were human kingdoms before the Hegemony absorbed them, conquered or coerced into clientage in the deep past, and the memory of having once been free ran under the client centuries like groundwater. The catastrophe freed them into a broken world, and they filled the vacuum faster than any other people could. The Merathine Empire — humanity's premature bid for the hegemony the Kynathi lost — has been receding for three centuries, but its legacy shapes Meratan politics more than any living institution. Humans exist in every climate and culture, from the tundra peoples of Isenfall to the highland clans of Teutania to the trading cities of the south, and the memory of client status colors human-Kynathi relations to this day — deference in some courts, and in others a quiet hunger to see the Dominion finished. Human religious life — the folk Courtesies, the imperial Kingsblood and Pactorate, and the answer-seeking movements grown since the catastrophe — is treated in a companion document.

The people of Isenfall are culturally distinct from southern humans: their ancestors crossed from the northern continent of Arlengh generations before the Spellbreak, and when the catastrophe sealed the northern ocean in storm and ice, the crossing became one-way and the homeland became a memory. Indigenous tribes that predated the Arlengh settlers have dwindled significantly.

### Giants
A race in decline — and the decline predates the catastrophe. In the deep past the Giants fought the **Giant War** against the Kynathi Hegemony, and it broke their strength generations before the Spellbreak ever came; what the war was over, and who could truly be said to have won it, survives only in fragments, and the Giants do not discuss it with outsiders. The Giants of the present mostly keep to their own, avoiding more prolific peoples except when defending territory. Some communities require tribute or offering from nearby settlements depending on longstanding local relationships. Their fuller history is largely lost — including whether they share stock with the giants of Arlengh, whose orphaned descendants still sail the world's oceans.

### Sea Giants
The Sea Giants are Arlengh's orphans. Their people originated on the northern continent, and when the Spellbreak sealed it in permanent storm and ice, those aboard the great hulks — fleets, colonies, or something else entirely — were left with nowhere to return. They have no land-based civilization: they live entirely aboard three known **sea-hulks**, vessels so vast they are mistaken for islands at distance, and some phantom islands on old charts are almost certainly hulk sightings no one understood. What happened on Arlengh — whether its giants and its human peoples destroyed one another, whether the ice took everyone, whether something worse occurred — is unknown and, with the crossing sealed, likely unknowable. Whether the hulks' endless circling is exile, mourning, or search has never been answered by anyone qualified to know.

An encounter is a generational event, less a raid than a natural disaster — the hurricane that comes once in a hundred years. Port cities keep hulk protocols that have never been tested in living memory, and the signs are remembered the way storms are: harbor birds going quiet, the swell turning wrong, and then a silhouette on the horizon that the lookout logs as an uncharted island — until it moves. When a hulk makes landfall it strips a city the way a whaler strips a whale — timber, metal, livestock, stores, and people — and departs without message, negotiation, or apparent malice. The reported "alliance" with the Myrathi is a misreading: Myrathi vessels are occasionally seen moving ahead of a hulk, heralds by omen rather than treaty — their Readings, not any compact, put them there.

### Reshai
A monkey-like people indigenous to the Pritani Jungle of southeastern Merata. Mostly keeping to themselves, the Reshai practice an animistic spiritual tradition centered on the veneration of nature spirits and their divine patron, **Jakanu** — a trickster deity associated with cleverness, martial wit, and the divine nature of the Reshai people. Masks are ritually significant in Reshai culture, worn for different spiritual and social purposes. Their martial tradition, practiced primarily with the staff, is associated with Jakanu's own legendary feats.

### Sehrati
The people of the Sahridi Desert — reclusive, mysterious, and rarely encountered beyond the desert's edges. They appear broadly human in form, with deep amber to dark brown skin, dark hair, and a notable tendency toward pale gold or amber eyes. Their dress is elaborate desert attire ornamented with patterns that echo the carvings found in the ruins they consider sacred; ritual scarification is common.

The Sahridi Desert was not always a desert. The Spellbreak transformed the region, burying the remains of the **Vahari** — an extinct civilization that predates all currently living peoples — beneath the sand. The ruins that protrude above the surface are treated by the Sehrati as sacred ground, approached with ritual and reverence. A priestly caste holds knowledge of deeper buried ruins: some chambers sealed since before memory, some entered and not spoken of again. The Sehrati do not know they may be the distant descendants of the Vahari. The connection has been entirely lost.

### The Made Peoples (Orcs, Goblins, Trolls, Ogres, and Kin)
The peoples that scholarship calls *made* are, by the most widely credited account, the surviving works of the **Vorathi**: a lost branch of the elven family — flesh-shapers who bred and built servitor peoples for labor and war in the deep pre-Spellbreak past. The Vorathi are extinct, and were extinct before the catastrophe. How they died is contested in the way everything important in Tessera is contested: some accounts say their creations rose against them; some say the other elven peoples destroyed them for practicing abomination; some say they made, or reached for, something that unmade them. Natari does not discuss the chapter at all — the Hegemony's records of the Vorathi, if they exist, have never been produced — and the silence is generally read as an answer of some kind.

Their former servitors outlived them, and in the vacuum after the Spellbreak proliferated across Merata's margins — the hills, the wastes, the eastern grasslands, the edges of every map the kingdoms draw. The made peoples' own traditions about their origin vary profoundly: some curse the makers by name, some venerate the freeing itself as the founding event, and some deny having been made at all, holding themselves peoples in their own right whose histories owe the Vorathi nothing. Their existence complicates the Urath ancestry debate in ways elven scholarship prefers not to examine — if the makers were elves, the made are, in some terrible sense, family. Legal and social standing varies enormously by kingdom; among the clans of the Vezhan Steppe, orcs and goblinoids ride as equals. The individual cultures of these peoples, and their individual relationships to the making, remain to be developed.

### The Riftborn
The youngest people in Merata — younger than the kingdoms, younger than most ruins. In the decades after the Spellbreak, children began to be born changed in communities near rifts, leyline fractures, and the Blightlands' edge: horns, tails, claws, eyes that see in darkness, skin and hair in colors nature never issued. The changes bred true. Seven centuries on, the riftborn are a people — thin-spread, mostly urban or frontier, common in Loneridge and the Blightland-facing settlements, rare and remarked-upon everywhere else.

They are the catastrophe made flesh, and are treated accordingly: blamed in bad years, scapegoated after incursions, barred from some cities and merely watched in others. The theological question that follows them is genuinely unsettled — whether they are Aberration-touched, a new people entire, or something the framework produced *on purpose* in its breaking. The riftborn themselves are not one culture and hold no single answer; "riftborn" is the common tongue's word, and the largest self-identified communities use names of their own. To be developed further.

### Vampires
In Tessera's metaphysics, life is participation in the weave, and blood is its living medium — the current through which a body carries its share of creation: vitality, selfhood, the continuity that makes a person the same person across their days. A vampire is a being whose own participation is broken — not severed cleanly, as death severs, but ruined and left open — and which persists by drinking the participation of others. What a vampire takes with blood is not nourishment but *being*: the victim survives diminished, memories thinned, their name a little harder to hold; heavy or repeated feeding can erase a person without ever killing them.

The blood must be warm and living — dead blood carries no current — and the need is perpetual, because a vampire does not feed to survive. A vampire is past surviving. It feeds to remain *itself*. Starved, a vampire does not die; it dissolves into un-being: memory goes first, then the name, then the shape of the self, until what remains is a hollowed thing of pure appetite — mindless, close kin to the Murmur, and far more dangerous to ordinary people than any deliberate lord of the night. Elder vampires fear the stake far less than they fear forgetting. And because blood carries memory, old blood is rich: what a vampire drinks from the long-lived it keeps in fragments — which makes the dying witnesses of Natari the most coveted vessels in the world. The Dominion guards its elders against more than time.

What made the first vampires is unknown — and the **First Night**, long told as their origin, is better understood as their greatest event. When the framework shattered, everyone then within the threshold of death — the world's dying, caught mid-crossing in the catastrophe's instant — was sundered half in and half out, unable to finish dying, unable to resume living. Across Merata, the deathbeds of that hour rose: a mass generation of every people, all of an age with the catastrophe, all remembering the world before — a second population of witnesses, undying where Natari's are dying, hidden where Natari's are guarded. But vampires existed before the breaking. The eldest are older than the catastrophe, their own origin lost even to themselves — starved and re-fed across the ages until the memory of what made them dissolved — and they regard the First Night generation the way old blood regards an infestation: too many, too young, made all at once by an accident rather than by whatever made *them*. Rival accounts of the deep origin credit a Daevic mark — hunger without terminus made flesh — or something older still; the truth may no longer exist anywhere, in any mind. Transmission is less disputed: a vampire's blood, given to the dying, reenacts the broken crossing in a single body — the First Night in miniature, and perhaps, some scholars note uneasily, a reenactment of something the First Night was itself reenacting. Kynathi doctrine classes the vampire as walking severance and kills it on sight; the Vigil's classification struggles, for the vampire is neither Eldritch nor Aberration but some third thing, and the struggle is recorded in the Codex's margins.

### Were-Creatures
Were-creatures exist across Tessera and are associated above all with Gloamwood, where the condition — if condition is the word — appears to breed true. Alnathi tradition connects them to Syrath's touch upon the world: completed transformations, the Becoming made lineage. The Codex raises the question and pointedly declines to settle it. To be developed further.

## Peoples Design Notes

*Out-of-world reference: what is true, what is belief, and what should stay unresolved.*

**True:** The Kynathi, Alnathi, Myrathi, and Dyrathi share enough ancient linguistic and cultural residue for the *-athi* problem to matter. The Kynathi Hegemony dominated much of pre-Spellbreak Merata and collapsed into the Natari Dominion. The Alnathi schism predates the Spellbreak. The Dyrathi are comparatively ascendant among the old peoples. Humans are the continent's plurality and fastest recovery population. The made peoples, riftborn, vampires, and were-creatures all exist as described here.

**Belief, not established reality:** Kynathi divine descent from the Urath. Any single account of the Urath or Elathi. The most widely credited Vorathi origin of all made peoples. Kynathi claims that Alnathi transformation is Syrath's mark. Alnathi claims that it is merely a Spellbreak scar. Outsider readings of the Myrathi, Reshai, Sehrati, and Sea Giants.

**Deliberately unresolved (recommend never confirming):** The exact nature of the Urath. Whether *-athi* names indicate literal shared lineage, linguistic inheritance, or later scholarly patterning. The full cause of the Vorathi extinction. Whether the Sehrati are Vahari descendants in any recoverable sense. The ultimate origin of vampires. Whether riftborn are Aberration-touched, a new people entire, or a purposeful outcome of the broken framework. The true cause of were-creatures breeding true in Gloamwood.

**Open hooks:** A made-people culture that treats the Vorathi as ancestral shame, divine murderers, or irrelevant slander. Riftborn self-names and institutions in Loneridge. Reshai mask-practice as a true identity craft. Sehrati chamber-rites beneath the Sahridi Desert. Dyrathi creditors deciding the fate of surface wars. Sea-hulk protocols finally tested. The last Kynathi witnesses becoming targets for vampires, scholars, and Natari factions.

**Guardrails:** Do not let the elven record become the whole record. Each people should eventually have at least one true practice or taxonomy that the Codex, Doctrine, and human Courtesies miss. Entries marked to be developed are placeholders, not invitations to fill with genre defaults.
