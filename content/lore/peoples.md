# Tessera — Peoples

*A companion document to the world reference. This file holds the elder-race theories, current peoples, made peoples, riftborn, vampires, and were-creatures formerly summarized in `world.md`.*

## The Elder Races (Urath)

Scholars and oral tradition across multiple cultures reference an ancient people who predated the current division of races. The academic term, reconstructed from linguistic fragments at Tindaire, is **Elathi** — meaning roughly "the first people." In older oral traditions, particularly among the Dyrathi, the name **Urath** survives. What can be recovered about them is fragmentary and contested.

The Kynathi claim direct divine descent from the Urath, positioning their own people as the truest inheritors of Tessera's original civilized lineage. In Kynathi theology, the Urath were not mortal in the way current peoples are mortal — they were the first divine inhabitants of the world, and the Kynathi's magical gifts are evidence of this inheritance. This claim is not accepted by all peoples.

## Races

### Kynathi (High Elves)
Before the Spellbreak the Kynathi were the continental power: the **Kynathi Hegemony**, a civilization of extraordinary craft, learning, and ambition whose reach — direct, or through client peoples — covered much of Merata. The catastrophe gutted it within a generation and what remains is the **Natari Dominion** (see [Geopolitics](geopolitics.md)). Long-lived and slow to breed — a Kynathi may see nine centuries — they have never recovered their numbers, and never will on any timescale that matters to their rivals.

They venerate the Avara, a pantheon of ancestor-gods, and believe themselves descended from the Urath, the divine first people of Tessera. Kynathi theology frames the Spellbreak as a wound to the divine order.


### Alnathi (Dark Elves)
The Alnathi and the Kynathi are one people twice divided — first by heresy, then by catastrophe — and the schism was about the Daeva from the beginning.

### Myrathi (Sea Elves)
The most visually and culturally distinct of the elven peoples. The Myrathi occupy the Shattered Isles in the Orathian Sea, and their impossibly fast warships are feared by all sailors — a Myrathi sail on the horizon is read as a death omen. The fear is earned but the reading is wrong, because there is no pattern for outsiders to read.

Myrathi culture turns on **the encounter**. Any meeting — a foreign vessel, a whale-run, a storm-wrack, a stranger cast ashore — must be acted upon as circumstance dictates, and circumstance is read in the moment by the ship's **tidecaller**: the winds, the water, the taste of salt and brine, the position of the stars or sun. The practice is called **the Reading**, and its verdict is absolute. The Myrathi do not raid, kill, pillage, or take captives for sport, plunder, or even survival; they do what the sea dictated. This is why no outsider ever learns why a Myrathi vessel killed all but one of a crew and carried him away, or disabled a ship and left it drifting, or drew alongside, did nothing, and departed. Someone taken by a Reading was not enslaved but *named by the sea* — some are returned years later, changed; some become Myrathi; some are delivered to a place and left there, for reasons no one, possibly including the Myrathi, will ever explain.

Whether something truly answers the Reading — the sea itself, something beneath it, something the Myrathi would not call a Daeva, or nothing at all — is unknown to outsiders and perhaps contested among the Myrathi themselves. Sailors of every nation keep countermeasures — offerings thrown overboard, silence on deck, hull markings — none of which verifiably work, which is worse than if they did.

### Dyrathi (Dwarves)
The Dyrathi are ancestrally linked to the other Elven races through the ancient Urath lineage. Unlike the Kynathi and Giants, they are not in obvious decline — if anything, the opposite: sheltered underground from a substantially surface catastrophe, the Dyrathi came through the Spellbreak the least damaged of the great peoples. They maintain substantial mountain holds, deep mines, and underground cities along the mountain range forming the northern edge of Merata's human heartland. 

Some Dyrathi holdings are integrated into Meratan geopolitics through trade and treaty; others are entirely self-contained, hostile to outsiders, and indifferent to whatever happens on the surface. Fully integrated Dyrathi living among human or other cultures are not uncommon. The Dyrathi are renowned craftspeople.

### Humans
Diverse, adaptable, and the continent's plurality: the most numerous people of Merata. Before the Spellbreak most human polities existed in the Kynathi Hegemony's shadow as clients, tributaries, and auxiliaries — but they had not begun that way. There were human kingdoms before the Hegemony absorbed them, conquered or coerced into clientage in the deep past. Humans exist in every climate and culture, from the tundra peoples of Isenfall to the highland clans of Teutania to the trading cities of the south.

The people of Isenfall are culturally distinct from southern humans: their ancestors crossed from the northern continent of Arlengh generations before the Spellbreak, and when the catastrophe sealed the northern ocean in storm and ice, the crossing became one-way and the homeland became a memory. Indigenous tribes that predated the Arlengh settlers have dwindled significantly.

### Giants
A race in decline — and the decline predates the catastrophe. In the deep past the Giants fought the **Giant War** against the Kynathi Hegemony, and it broke their strength generations before the Spellbreak ever came; what the war was over, and who could truly be said to have won it, survives only in fragments, and the Giants do not discuss it with outsiders. The Giants of the present mostly keep to their own, avoiding more prolific peoples except when defending territory. Some communities require tribute or offering from nearby settlements depending on longstanding local relationships. Their fuller history is largely lost — including whether they share stock with the giants of Arlengh.

### Sea Giants
The Sea Giants are Arlengh's orphans. Their people originated on the northern continent, and when the Spellbreak sealed it in permanent storm and ice, those aboard the great hulks — fleets, colonies, or something else entirely — were left with nowhere to return. They have no land-based civilization: they live entirely aboard three known **sea-hulks**, vessels so vast they are mistaken for islands at distance, and some phantom islands on old charts are almost certainly hulk sightings no one understood. What happened on Arlengh — whether its giants and its human peoples destroyed one another, whether the ice took everyone, whether something worse occurred — is unknown and, with the crossing sealed, likely unknowable. Whether the hulks' endless circling is exile, mourning, or search has never been answered by anyone qualified to know.

An encounter is a generational event, less a raid than a natural disaster — the hurricane that comes once in a hundred years. Port cities keep hulk protocols that have never been tested in living memory, and the signs are remembered the way storms are: harbor birds going quiet, the swell turning wrong, and then a silhouette on the horizon that the lookout logs as an uncharted island — until it moves. When a hulk makes landfall it strips a city the way a whaler strips a whale — timber, metal, livestock, stores, and people — and departs without message, negotiation, or apparent malice. The reported "alliance" with the Myrathi is a misreading: Myrathi vessels are occasionally seen moving ahead of a hulk, heralds by omen rather than treaty — their Readings, not any compact, put them there.

### Reshai
A monkey-like people indigenous to the Pritani Jungle of southeastern Merata. Mostly keeping to themselves, the Reshai practice an animistic spiritual tradition centered on the veneration of nature spirits and their divine patron, **Jakanu** — a trickster deity associated with cleverness, martial wit, and the divine nature of the Reshai people. Masks are ritually significant in Reshai culture, worn for different spiritual and social purposes. Their martial tradition, practiced primarily with the staff, is associated with Jakanu's own legendary feats.

### Sehrati
The people of the Sahridi Desert — reclusive, mysterious, and rarely encountered beyond the desert's edges. They appear broadly human in form, with deep amber to dark brown skin, dark hair, and a notable tendency toward pale gold or amber eyes. Their dress is elaborate desert attire ornamented with patterns that echo the carvings found in the ruins they consider sacred; ritual scarification is common.

The Sahridi Desert was not always a desert. The Spellbreak transformed the region, burying the remains of an extinct civilization that predates all currently living peoples beneath the sand. The ruins that protrude above the surface are treated by the Sehrati as sacred ground, approached with ritual and reverence.

### The Riftborn
The youngest people in Merata. In the decades after the Spellbreak, children began to be born changed in communities near rifts, leyline fractures, and blighted lands: horns, tails, claws, eyes that see in darkness, skin and hair in colors nature never issued. The changes bred true. The riftborn are a people — thin-spread, mostly urban or frontier, rare and remarked-upon everywhere else.

They are the catastrophe made flesh, and are treated accordingly: blamed in bad years, scapegoated after incursions, barred from some cities and merely watched in others. The theological question that follows them is genuinely unsettled — whether they are Aberration-touched, a new people entire, or something the framework produced *on purpose* in its breaking. The riftborn themselves are not one culture and hold no single answer; "riftborn" is the common tongue's word, and the largest self-identified communities use names of their own.

### Vampires
In Tessera's metaphysics, life is participation in the weave, and blood is its living medium — the current through which a body carries its share of creation: vitality, selfhood, the continuity that makes a person the same person across their days. A vampire is a being whose own participation is broken — not severed cleanly, as death severs, but ruined and left open — and which persists by drinking the participation of others. What a vampire takes with blood is not nourishment but *being*: the victim survives diminished, memories thinned, their name a little harder to hold; heavy or repeated feeding can erase a person without ever killing them.

The blood must be warm and living — dead blood carries no current — and the need is perpetual, because a vampire does not feed to survive. A vampire is past surviving. It feeds to remain *itself*. Starved, a vampire does not die; it dissolves into un-being: memory goes first, then the name, then the shape of the self, until what remains is a hollowed thing of pure appetite — mindless, close kin to the Murmur, and far more dangerous to ordinary people than any deliberate lord of the night. Elder vampires fear the stake far less than they fear forgetting. And because blood carries memory, old blood is rich: what a vampire drinks from the long-lived it keeps in fragments — which makes the dying witnesses of Natari the most coveted vessels in the world. The Dominion guards its elders against more than time.

What made the first vampires is unknown — and the **First Night**, long told as their origin, is better understood as their greatest event. When the framework shattered, everyone then within the threshold of death — the world's dying, caught mid-crossing in the catastrophe's instant — was sundered half in and half out, unable to finish dying, unable to resume living. Across Merata, the deathbeds of that hour rose: a mass generation of every people, all of an age with the catastrophe, all remembering the world before — a second population of witnesses, undying where Natari's are dying, hidden where Natari's are guarded. But vampires existed before the breaking. The eldest are older than the catastrophe, their own origin lost even to themselves — starved and re-fed across the ages until the memory of what made them dissolved — and they regard the First Night generation the way old blood regards an infestation: too many, too young, made all at once by an accident rather than by whatever made *them*. Rival accounts of the deep origin credit a Daevic mark — hunger without terminus made flesh — or something older still; the truth may no longer exist anywhere, in any mind. Transmission is less disputed: a vampire's blood, given to the dying, reenacts the broken crossing in a single body — the First Night in miniature, and perhaps, some scholars note uneasily, a reenactment of something the First Night was itself reenacting. Kynathi doctrine classes the vampire as walking severance and kills it on sight; the Vigil's classification struggles, for the vampire is neither Eldritch nor Aberration but some third thing, and the struggle is recorded in the Codex's margins.

### Were-Creatures
Were-creatures exist across Tessera and are associated above all with Gloamwood, where the condition — if condition is the word — appears to breed true. Alnathi tradition connects them to Syrath's touch upon the world: completed transformations, the Becoming made lineage. The Codex raises the question and pointedly declines to settle it.
