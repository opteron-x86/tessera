# The Nerebine Codex

*A companion document to the Tessera world reference. The Codex is the in-world text through which literate Merata understands the Graveland. Its schema is influential, coherent, and wrong in ways that matter.*

---

## Overview

The Nerebine Codex is the foundational mortal work of Daevic scholarship — the source of nearly everything the literate peoples of Merata believe about the Graveland and its inhabitants. Compiled by the Alnathi death-priests of Nerebyasca from centuries of vigil records, the Codex imposed a structure on the Graveland that no one had articulated before it and that no serious scholarship has escaped since: the schema of **Seven and One** — seven Great Daeva enthroned, one throne hollow, rendered as an eight-pointed sigil with the eighth point drawn dark.

The schema is the Codex's invention. The Graveland is messier. Whether the Codex merely simplified that mess, or whether — as its most disturbing critics contend — the act of codification began *reshaping* the Graveland to match the map, is the deepest open question in Daevic study.

## Origins

### The Vigil of Nerebyasca

The Alnathi death-priests who keep the necropolis at Nerebyasca are collectively called **the Vigil**. Their order predates the Codex by an unknown span — perhaps by the whole age of the Alnathi exile, for the Vigil neither confirms nor denies descent from the founding schools of the Anchorite heresy, and its charge at the boundary is consistent with both of that heresy's old doctrines, the harbor and the mooring. Their original charge was funerary — tending the Alnathi dead and the boundary that the dead must cross — and it was in this work, conducted on the edge of the Blightlands where the boundary is thinnest, that they accumulated the raw material of the Codex: generations of records describing what pressed against that boundary from the other side, what it said, what it wanted, and what it cost to answer.

### Ashvenn the Twice-Grieved

Tradition attributes the Codex's redaction to a single figure: **Ashvenn the Twice-Grieved**, a death-priest of the Vigil. The epithet is explained two ways. The pious account holds that she grieved once for a child lost to a rift incursion and once for the whole of the living world, when her studies revealed to her what waits outside it. The skeptical account — recorded in Tindaire marginalia — holds that the second grief came not from what she learned but from what she *did*: that Ashvenn understood, before she finished, what fixing names to nameless things would feed, and completed the work anyway because the alternative was ignorance.

Ashvenn's method was ruthless reduction. The vigil records described hundreds of entities, contradictory encounters, beings that seemed to be several things at once or nothing consistently. Ashvenn discarded, merged, and ranked until a legible structure remained. The Codex's own preface — one of the few passages identical in every recension — does not apologize for this:

> *"In the Graveland there is no law, no crown, and no oath. There is only memory, and the hunger to be remembered. Rank is coherence; the name held whole is the only throne. I have charted the names that held. Of the rest, this book is the tombstone."*

### The Recensions

Three lineages of the text exist, and the differences between them are part of its scholarship.

**The Vigil Original** remains at Nerebyasca and has never been copied in full. It is consulted under rite, by permission of the Vigil, in the presence of a death-priest, and readers report that it contains chapters no copy reproduces (see *The Withheld Chapters*). The Vigil maintains and re-corrects the Original by hand; certain passages must be re-inserted every generation because scribes reliably fail to preserve them (see *the Vigil of Karath*, below).

**The Tindaire Recension** is the scholarly standard across Merata — the version cited, taught, and argued over. It was produced under negotiated agreement between the Vigil and the college, is annotated with two centuries of Tindaire commentary, and is incomplete *by design*: the Vigil withheld chapters at copying, and the Recension marks each omission with an empty ruled frame, so that the reader always knows the book has holes.

**The vulgates** are the corrupt popular copies — abridged, mistranslated, embellished, and traded in the same black markets that move unlicensed Loneridge goods through Cassidor and Seradon. The Vigil regards vulgates not merely as bad scholarship but as *dangerous objects*, for reasons rooted in the Codex's own doctrine: a corrupted name is still a name, and a name repeated is a name fed. There are documented cases — the Vigil claims — of vulgate errors stabilizing into something that answers. Among death-priests this has a proverb: *scribal error is midwifery.*

## The Schema of Seven and One

### The Sigil

The Codex organizes the Graveland as an eight-pointed sigil. Seven points bear names: the **Enthroned**, the Great Daeva. The eighth point is drawn dark, and depending on the copy is left blank, inked black, or scratched through — the **Hollow Throne**. The Codex is explicit that eight is the *shape* of the hierarchy and seven is its living count: the structure is visibly incomplete, a wound in its own geometry.

### The Three Tiers

**The Enthroned** (Codex: *the Seated Names*). Daeva whose identity is coherent enough to be self-sustaining — a name, a nature, and a history held whole across ages. Coherence is their power, and each generates an orbit.

**The Named** (Codex: *the Lesser Names*). Daeva of partial identity — a single obsession, a fragment of something dead, a self built around one idea. Too incomplete to stand alone, they fall into orbit around an Enthroned whose nature resonates with theirs. They are not sworn; they are *caught*, held by gravity rather than fealty. A Named that grows — through cults, invocation, fear, the simple traffic of mortal naming — can drift between orbits, consume its peers, or in rare ages usurp a throne. Every court is therefore also a slow siege.

**The Murmur** (Codex: *the Unlettered*). The nameless mass — hungers, impulses, and remnants with no identity at all, incapable of coherent action in the world. The Murmur is the background dread of rifts, the whispering in Blightland dreams, and the raw material from which Named occasionally condense. The Codex teaches that most "demons" encountered by mortals are Murmur wearing a borrowed moment of shape, and that treating such an encounter as a *being* — naming it, bargaining with it, recording it — risks making it one.

### A Note on Titles

The ranks of "prince," "duke," and "count" that pervade Merathine demonology do not appear in the Codex. They are artifacts of the Tindaire Recension's translation into Merathine court-language, in which the translators — needing terms for gradations of the Lesser Names — borrowed the peerage of the empire. The Vigil's terms do not translate cleanly and describe degrees of *coherence*, not station. The feudal metaphor has nonetheless conquered the popular imagination so completely that even Alnathi practitioners outside Nerebyasca use it, to the Vigil's quiet contempt.

## The Seven Vigils

The Codex's chapters are called **Vigils**, each addressing one of the Enthroned and its orbit. Entries below summarize the Codex's teaching together with the principal disputes attached to each.

### The Vigil of Leraye — The Perfect Act

Precision, mastery, and the fracture that mastery produces. The Codex's teaching on Leraye is inseparable from its teaching on Leraye's court: **Iphareth** (perfection turned inward, upon the self) and **Chayliel** (perfection of pursuit — the hunt that survives its own quarry). The Vigil preserves the old Alnathi doctrine that these three were once a single being that perfected itself until it broke, which would make Leraye's court not a court at all but the orbiting shards of its own shattering. The Codex records — without endorsing — that Iphareth and Chayliel each hold themselves to be the true original, and that Leraye has never addressed the question. A Tindaire annotation adds the obvious, unanswerable corollary: if the doctrine is true, then the Enthroned of the perfect act is itself a fragment, and the sigil's first point is a fiction three times over.

### The Vigil of Vaurun — The Revelation

Knowledge that is true and costs everything. The Codex is emphatic that Vaurun does not lie and treats this as the ground of its danger rather than a mitigation of it. In orbit: **Azdrath** (ambition, overreach), which the Codex styles Vaurun's *herald* — the whisper that makes a mind ready to receive what Vaurun offers. The Vigil's account of the Spellbreak's theft-of-divinity theory runs through this pair: it was Azdrath that carried the ritual to mortal ears, and the Codex pointedly declines to say whether the herald acted on its principal's behalf or overreached — noting only that overreach is Azdrath's nature, and that this is exactly what Vaurun would rely upon.

The chapter lists one further member of the court that no copy names. The Tindaire Recension renders it as an empty cartouche — a ruled frame where a name should be — glossed only as *the keeper of what is sealed: the count whose name is written but not to be read.* The Vigil Original is said to contain the name. No reader has repeated it.

### The Vigil of Nymeris — The Veil

Desire, misdirection, the beautiful untruth. The Codex concedes in its opening lines that this chapter is the one most likely to be wrong, since its subject's nature is to be misapprehended. In orbit: **Orryn** (false memory, the rewritten past). The Orryn passage is the Codex's famous textual scandal: no two copies of it agree, collation attempts fail in ways that embarrass their authors, and Tindaire has for generations assigned the task to first-year scholars as a form of hazing. The Vigil's marginalia preserve a stranger claim — that Orryn originated in Vaurun's court and *fled*: the lie that escaped the truth. Whether Vaurun wants it back, the margin does not say. It is the only place in the Codex where one Enthroned is recorded as having a grievance touching another's orbit.

### The Vigil of Mahadron — The Yoke

Dominion, command, the will that admits no other. The Codex's chapter on Mahadron is the most orderly in the book: its court is enumerated in perfect symmetrical ranks — among them **Vhastur, the Kneeling Voice** (the compulsion to obey that arrives dressed as one's own thought) and **Dravask the Surrendered** (the relief of yielding, the sweetness of the laid-down will) — and the chapter carries no marginal disputes whatsoever. The Tindaire annotators find this more unsettling than any contested passage, and say so: every other Vigil is a record of argument, because every other court is alive with drift and siege. Nothing in Mahadron's orbit grows, because Mahadron permits no growth beneath it. The Named it has caught are the ones, the Codex observes, that other Daeva pity — *if Daeva pity.*

### The Vigil of Selveth — The Hunger

Appetite without terminus. In orbit: **Velmis** (proliferation, plague, mindless spread) — hunger's outward expression, consumption become contagion, which the Codex treats less as a subject than as a symptom. The chapter's most striking feature is that most of it is a necrology: the Codex lists more dead names under Selveth than under all other Vigils combined — Named that fell into its orbit and, over ages, simply ceased to be distinguishable from it. The Vigil's teaching is that Selveth's court is not a hierarchy but a digestion, and the chapter closes on the book's bleakest line: *"Of the names below, nothing is written because nothing remains. This page is their monument, and it is more than they kept of themselves."*

### The Vigil of Karath — The End

Inevitability, entropy, the reminder that all things stop. In orbit: **Nuvaal** (erasure, silence — the ending of even memory) and **Thessavane** (grief — the shadow the end casts forward).

The Nuvaal passage is the Codex's shortest, and it shrinks. Scribes reliably omit lines from it; copies verifiably lose text across generations; the Vigil re-inserts the missing sentences into the Original at every recension, from records kept — it is said — in a form that is not writing. The Tindaire Recension prints the passage inside a black border with a standing instruction that any scholar who notices the text has shortened must report it. Several have. The Codex observes that even Karath is careful with Nuvaal: erasure is the one thing inevitability cannot survive.

The Thessavane passage is the only place the Codex addresses a Daeva with something resembling compassion. The Vigil's teaching — heterodox everywhere else, doctrine at Nerebyasca — is that Thessavane's divine memory is *true*: that it alone among the catalogued powers was a genuine Creator, whose world failed and died, and whose obsessive tending of mortal grief is not predation but projection. It orbits Karath by choice — the only Named in the Graveland recorded as being where it is voluntarily, keeping company with the principle that took everything from it. The chapter notes its particular attention to the Kynathi without comment.

### The Vigil of Syrath — The Becoming

Transformation that cannot be undone; the revelation of what one always was. The Codex treats Syrath as the most tolerated of the Enthroned and the one the Alnathi tradition engages most openly. Its court is small and strange — Named of thresholds, of molting, of the moment between shapes — and its orbit is the loosest of the seven, because Syrath alone among the Enthroned does not mind when things in its orbit change into something that leaves. The chapter raises and pointedly declines to settle what it calls *the Gloamwood question*: whether the were-creatures of that forest are the residue of Syrath's touch upon Tessera, a lineage of completed transformations breeding true. The Vigil's refusal to answer is generally read as an answer.

## The Eighth Vigil — The Hollow Throne

The Codex's final chapter addresses the dark point of the sigil, and opens with its most quoted passage:

> *"The throne is not empty. Empty implies it waits. It is hollow: what sat there was scooped out, and the shape of the sitting remains."*

The Vigil's teaching: an eighth Enthroned existed — a Great Daeva of **fire** — and is gone. Consumed, burned out, or unmade so completely that even its erasure left no edges. The Codex does not record the dead name. Readers have long argued whether this is discretion or incapacity — whether the Vigil withholds the name, or whether the name genuinely cannot be recovered, which would carry the fingerprints of Nuvaal and imply things about the Graveland's history that the Codex declines to imply.

The orbit persists, and two powerful Named contest the dark point:

**Razael** — fire as *renewal*: overthrow, revolution, the burning that clears dead growth so something new can live. **Phoros** — fire as *purification*: zeal, crusade, the flame as divine mandate. Each claims to be the Eighth returned. Each may sincerely believe it. Neither can prove it — possibly not even to itself. Their contest is the one open war the Codex records in the Graveland, fought almost entirely through mortal proxies: Razael's revolutions against Phoros's crusades, grievance against righteousness, and coastlines of ash where their cults collide. The Codex's driest annotation belongs to this chapter: mortals caught between the two fires never learn that both issue from the same hollow seat.

One line in the Vigil Original does not appear in any copy. It was seen by a Tindaire delegate during a supervised consultation and recorded from memory: a marginal note, struck through by a later hand, recording a *third* claimant to the Hollow Throne — one that had been mortal. The Vigil, asked about the passage, replied that the Original contains many struck lines, and that they are struck for reasons.

## The Withheld Chapters

The Vigil Original contains material absent from every copy, marked in the Tindaire Recension by empty ruled frames. From the placement and size of the omissions, and from fragments of testimony by permitted readers, scholarship has inferred at least three withheld chapters:

**The Sealed Vigil** — believed to concern *assumption*: the doctrine that a mortal who takes up a Daevic name completely — the deeds, the nature, the cult, the story — can accrete into the thing named. The one fragment attributed to it, repeated in whispers for generations, is doctrinally consistent with everything the Codex teaches openly: *"The name does not care what wears it."* Whether the chapter is scholarship, warning, or instruction is the most dangerous open question attached to the book.

**The Count of Thrones** — believed to address how many hollow thrones there are, and to give a number other than one.

**The Complete Nuvaal** — believed to exist. Believed, by the more careful annotators, to be the reason the Vigil re-corrects the Original by hand rather than trusting any scribe.

**The Mooring** — inferred most recently and disputed most heavily: a chapter believed to concern the oldest Anchorite doctrine — whether souls bound to the Daeva anchor those powers to the living world, and the world itself against its ending. If the chapter exists, the Vigil's silence about it is the most consequential silence in Merata.

Tindaire has petitioned for the withheld chapters at intervals for two centuries. The Vigil's refusals have varied in courtesy but not in substance.

## Errors, Heresies, and Counter-Traditions

### The Kynathi Counter-Tradition

Kynathi theology rejects the Codex root and branch. In the Natari teaching, the Daeva are **the Severed** — dead things cut off from creation, without hierarchy, without individuality worth the name, and the entire enterprise of cataloguing them is not scholarship but *feeding*: every name fixed, every rank assigned, every copy made presses coherence into things that should be left to dissolve. The Codex is, in the Natari formulation, an atrocity of three hundred pages.

The bitter irony, noted quietly even at Tindaire: the Kynathi are substantially correct about the mechanism. The Codex's own doctrine — rank is memory, naming feeds — concedes it. The Alnathi position is not that the feeding is harmless but that ignorance is more dangerous than the meal: the boundary is already broken, the things beyond it already reach through, and a charted danger can be practiced against. The two traditions have never reconciled and will not. Each holds the other responsible, in part, for the state of the world.

### The Anomaly Records

Tindaire maintains a running collection of field reports that the schema cannot accommodate, catalogued with visible institutional discomfort. Among them: encounters with Named that answer coherently to *two* orbits at once; entities matching no Vigil that nonetheless display Enthroned-grade coherence; possession cases in which the possessing intelligence changed identity mid-habitation without changing behavior; and the stubborn testimony of Blightland-edge communities whose lived demonology simply does not map to eight points. The annotators' standing conclusion is the honest one: the sigil is a chart of the shore drawn from a ship in fog, and the fog is not incidental — it is the subject's medium.

### The Drowned Recension

Persistent tradition holds that Ashvenn's first complete draft enumerated a different count of Seated Names — more than seven; in some tellings, many more — and that the Vigil of her day rejected it, whereupon the draft was destroyed, sunk, or sealed. The story is unverifiable and the Vigil does not discuss it. Its significance is what it implies either way: if false, it shows how naturally readers suspect the seven-and-one of being a choice rather than a discovery. If true, it *confirms* it.

### The Cartographer's Heresy

The deepest controversy in Daevic scholarship, whispered at Tindaire and forbidden at Nerebyasca, follows the Codex's own doctrine to its conclusion. If rank is memory, and mortal naming feeds coherence, then five centuries of the Codex's readership — thousands of minds holding the same seven names, the same eight-pointed shape, generation upon generation — is the largest sustained act of collective naming in the history of the world. The heresy holds that the Graveland was formless, or differently formed, before the Codex; that the seven-and-one did not describe the Enthroned but *enthroned* them; that the map made the territory.

The heresy's corollary is worse than its thesis: whoever revises the Codex revises the Graveland. This, its adherents argue, explains everything about the Vigil's behavior that courtesy cannot — the uncopyable Original, the withheld chapters, the hand-corrections, the horror of vulgates. On this reading the Nerebine Codex is not a book of scholarship at all. It is an instrument that is still running, and the Vigil of Nerebyasca is not its librarian but its operator.

The Vigil has never dignified the heresy with rebuttal. Its adherents consider this significant. Its critics observe that the Vigil dignifies nothing with rebuttal, and that this is the entire problem with arguing about Nerebyasca.

## Handling, Reading, and Rite

The Vigil prescribes protocols for engagement with the text, observed strictly at Nerebyasca, loosely at Tindaire, and not at all by vulgate readers — which, the death-priests note, is the point of the protocols. Names from the Codex are not to be spoken aloud without occasion; reading is to be done in daylight or by flame, never at a rift's edge or in the Blightlands; a Vigil is to be read whole or not begun, since a name half-learned is held to be a door half-opened. Whether these rules reflect real metaphysical hazard or the accumulated superstition of a funerary order is debated by people who, it is generally observed, keep the rules anyway.

The Codex's closing line is the same in every recension, every vulgate, every fragment — the one sentence that no scribe has ever corrupted, which the Vigil regards as the book's only miracle and its critics regard as its author's last joke:

> *"All that is dead wants a name. Reader: so does all that lives. That is the whole of the danger, and you have carried it with you into this book, and now you carry it out."*

## Behind the Codex — Design Notes

*Out-of-world reference: what is true, what is belief, and what should stay unresolved.*

**True (Graveland reality):** Rank is memory — identity coherence is genuinely the substance of Daevic power. Accretion, drift, consumption of peers, and usurpation genuinely occur. Naming and worship genuinely feed coherence; the Kynathi are right about the mechanism. Mortal transcendence via assumption of a name is genuinely possible; whether the mortal becomes the Daeva or the name acquires a new mask is indistinguishable from outside and possibly from inside. Thessavane's memory is true. At least one Great-tier identity of fire existed and is gone.

**Codex schema, not reality:** The precise count of seven. The tidy assignment of every Named to exactly one orbit. The feudal titles (pure translation artifact). The assumption that the Hollow Throne is singular — there are more hollow thrones than one, and no reliable way to count them, since Nuvaal-adjacent erasure removes the evidence of what it removes.

**Deliberately unresolved (recommend never confirming):** The Cartographer's Heresy — whether the Codex partially *created* the seven-and-one by concentrating mortal belief. The strongest version of the setting keeps this permanently ambiguous: the Graveland was messier before the Codex and is *somewhat less messy now*, and no one, including the Vigil, can prove causation. Also unresolved: whether Razael or Phoros is the Eighth returned, whether either is the struck-through mortal claimant, the contents of the Sealed Vigil, and what the Vigil of Nerebyasca actually knows. Equally unresolved, and load-bearing: the Anchorite claims — the cycle of creations, the Appointed End, and the mooring. The Graveland's existence is genuine evidence for the cycle; nothing is evidence for how much time the world has; and whether seven centuries of Alnathi soul-binding has actually held Tessera against its ending is a question the setting should never answer.

**Guardrail (setting-wide):** The Codex is the loudest voice, not the deepest. Vocabulary is not ownership — *Avara*/*Daeva* are elven loanwords spread through Tindaire, and every major non-elven tradition (Sehrati, Reshai, Myrathi, human compact-religion, and others still to be written) keeps its own taxonomy that does not reduce to the doublet or to the seven-and-one. When developing those traditions, each should know at least one true thing the Codex does not.

**Open hooks:** The empty cartouche in Vaurun's court (a sealed-knowledge Named, designed for later reveal). The Drowned Recension (a physical MacGuffin — if it exists and enumerates the Graveland differently, the Cartographer's Heresy says finding it is not an archaeological event but a cosmological one). Vulgate-born Named (minor Daeva accidentally midwifed by scribal error — good low-tier antagonists with inherently unstable identities). The Vigil itself as a faction: librarians, operators, or jailers, depending on who is asking.
