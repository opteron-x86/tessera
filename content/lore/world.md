# Tessera — World Lore

---

## The World

Tessera is a world in decline, haunted by the memory of what it was. The continent of Merata is the center of current civilization — a patchwork of human kingdoms, diminished ancient peoples, scattered ruins, and places where old wounds in the fabric of reality have never fully healed. Life continues; people build, trade, war, and survive. But something essential is absent from the world's foundations. Magic is less predictable than it once was. The gods do not answer. The great works of a vanished golden age stand as evidence of what has been lost, and nothing of comparable scale has risen to replace them. The catastrophe is recent enough to touch: seven and a half centuries past, within a long elven lifetime — and the last living witnesses of the world before are dying now, one by one.

---

## The Waning

The Spellbreak did not strike a world at noon. For at least a century before the breaking — longer, by some readings — the world was already running down, in an age the surviving records call the Waning. The Avara answered less and less; their last confirmed manifestation was generations before the end, and the great temples had already begun the quiet transition from communion to commemoration. Magic cost more: workings that had been routine demanded effort, then ritual, then went unattempted. Births thinned among the long-lived peoples. The Hegemony's final age was its most magnificent and its most frightened — monuments raised at unprecedented scale by a civilization that had begun, without saying so, to behave like a household putting its affairs in order.

The Waning is the missing motive in every Spellbreak theory. Whatever was attempted at the end — the arrogant ritual, the theft of divinity, the reckless expansion of workings — was attempted by people watching their world dim, and desperation explains what hubris alone never quite did. The Anchorite heretics, cast out millennia earlier for preaching that creation must end, spent the Waning being slowly, unbearably vindicated. And the Appointed End theory rests its entire case upon it: a scheduled dissolution, its adherents observe, would look exactly like a Waning.

The golden age remembered today is, in part, a construction of the aftermath — grief-polished, curated at Natari, tended (the Codex would note) by a power that feeds on exactly this kind of longing, and gilded even by honest witnesses, in the way the old have always gilded their youth. Some of the dying rememberers know their memories are burnished and burnish them anyway. This is among the things that makes the Testimony at Tindaire so fraught: it may be recording history, or laundering a myth at the moment of its final revision.

A last principle, for scholars and readers alike: the Spellbreak explains less than it is asked to. Post-catastrophe tradition attributes everything to the breaking — it is the explanation that ends inquiry — and careful scholarship keeps finding that half of what folk memory blames on the Spellbreak is older, or other, or still unexplained. The catastrophe was the greatest event in the world's history. It was not the only one.

## The Spellbreak

Seven and a half centuries ago, the Spellbreak shattered the hidden framework through which the Creators shaped and sustained the world. The catastrophe severed their connection to creation, changed the laws of magic, weakened the boundaries between planes, and left portions of reality permanently wounded. All that remains are ruins, contradictory histories, unstable magic, silent gods, and a world that continues to function only because fragments of the old design still hold.

The prevailing theories, debated at Tindaire and elsewhere:

- **Kynathi Arrogance** — The Kynathi, at the height of their power, attempted something their magic could not sustain: a ritual to command creation itself, or to elevate their own kind to divine status. In Natari, this theory is suppressed.
- **Human Expansion** — Reckless mortal growth and unchecked resource extraction put unsustainable strain on the framework of creation. Human kingdoms have always found this theory convenient.
- **Magic Exhaustion** — The cumulative weight of centuries of large-scale magical use gradually degraded the underlying structure of the world until it gave way.
- **The Theft of Divinity** — Someone attempted a ritual to tear divine power from the Creators themselves and failed catastrophically. Taken seriously at Tindaire. A darker reading, current since the Nerebine Codex, holds that the ritual was an ascension attempt at the wrong scale — mortals trying to accrete not into a Daeva but into a Creator — and that the framework shattered under the claim.
- **Daevic Interference** — The most disturbing of the accepted theories: the Graveland powers had a hand in engineering the Spellbreak, or in making it worse, in order to shatter the framework that held them at bay.
- **The Appointed End** — The heresy the other theories exist to avoid: creation was never meant to be permanent. Worlds are made, run their course, and are ended — the Graveland is where their makers go — and the Spellbreak was not an accident but the beginning of Tessera's scheduled dissolution. On this reading, the world has spent seven centuries outliving its own end. The theory descends from the pre-Spellbreak Anchorite heresy preserved in the oldest Alnathi tradition; at Tindaire it is not debated so much as mentioned, and then not pursued.

No theory is universally accepted. Most historians acknowledge that the truth, if recoverable at all, is buried under seven and a half centuries of contested history — a short span, as burials go, which is itself suggestive: the past here is not merely lost, but in places deliberately unmade.

## The Graveland

Beyond mortal reality lies a dead astral expanse called the Graveland, inhabited by powers that never became true Creators, or whose creations failed and died. They are divine intelligences without living worlds — severed from the mortal connection that gives divinity purpose and form. For ages they pressed against the framework of reality from outside — and the framework resisted. It was never a wall: even in the world's golden age the Daeva could reach through, at great cost, into the rare thin place or the exceptionally receptive mind. But contact was difficult, dangerous, and opposed — by the framework itself and, the old traditions insist, by the Avara in person. The Spellbreak broke that resistance. Now, through rifts, dreams, broken leylines, and reckless magic, the failed gods reach into the world with an ease they never had while its makers still answered.

Mortals call them demons, devils, false gods, angels, saints, or the real gods returned. The elven peoples call them the Daeva — a word older than the schism that divides those peoples, and older than the catastrophe itself. None of those names are quite right. The beings themselves may no longer remember what they were. They only know that the world is alive, and they are not.

The Graveland also gives rise to creatures — entities native to the dead expanse that find their way into Tessera through rifts or summoning. Scholars categorize these as **Eldritch**, to distinguish them from **Aberrations**: creatures native to Tessera that have been corrupted or mutated by Spellbreak energies or other forces.

### The Two Words

In the oldest recoverable layer of the elven tongue, divinity came in two classes, and the distinction survives as a doublet: the **Avara** — "those who joined," the powers that bound themselves to the making and sustaining of Tessera — and the **Daeva** — "those who did not." The pre-Spellbreak religion knew both classes as present, active realities: the near gods who answered, and the far powers that pressed against the weave from outside. Both words outlived the world that religion belonged to, and their meanings diverged with the peoples who kept them. In Kynathi usage *Daeva* froze into a category of the rejected — which is why even Natari's Doctrine of the Severed permits the word: a category is not a name, and this one, orthodoxy holds, was handed down by the Avara themselves. In Alnathi usage it remained working vocabulary — a neutral label for powers one can have a relationship with. Both peoples claim the word. Tindaire's linguists point to the doublet as evidence that it belongs to neither and predates both.

### The Economy of Names

What passes for order in the Graveland is not law but coherence. Identity — a name, a nature, a history held whole — is the only substance of power among the dead, and the hunger to be remembered is the closest thing the Graveland has to a physics. Daeva coherent enough to sustain a self across ages are its great powers. Beneath them drift lesser Daeva of partial identity — a single obsession, a fragment of something dead, a self built around one idea — too incomplete to stand alone, falling into orbit around greater powers whose natures resonate with their own: not sworn, but *caught*, held by gravity rather than fealty. Beneath everything is the **Murmur**: the nameless mass of hungers, impulses, and remnants with no identity at all, incapable of coherent action in the world, felt only as the background dread of rifts and the whispering in Blightland dreams. Most "demons" encountered by mortals are Murmur wearing a borrowed moment of shape.

Because identity is power, **naming feeds**. Worship, invocation, fear, scholarship — any sustained mortal attention presses coherence into what it regards. A lesser Daeva with a growing cult can accrete into something greater; orbits decay and are contested; every court is also a slow siege, and in rare ages a throne is usurped. Great Daeva thus have concrete reasons to cultivate their own worship and starve their rivals' — which makes Daevic cult conflict in Merata a proxy war among the dead.

The chart through which literate Merata understands all of this is the **Nerebine Codex**, compiled by the Alnathi death-priests of Nerebyasca: seven Great Daeva enthroned, one throne hollow, rendered as an eight-pointed sigil with the eighth point drawn dark. The chart is the best map mortals have. It is tidier than the territory — and there are more hollow thrones than one, though Nuvaal's work removes the evidence of what it removes, and no reliable count exists.

### Daeva Contact

Contact predates the catastrophe. In the age of living orthodoxy the Avara were near — present, answering, on rare occasions manifest — and the Daeva were far, reaching through only at great cost. Daeva cults existed under the old order: forbidden, suppressed, rare, and real, drawing those who wanted what the Avara would not give — transformation outside the sanctioned order, knowledge the weave withheld, power without the obligations of participation. The Spellbreak inverted the distances: the Creators fell silent, and the dead drew close. The wrong gods became the reachable ones. That single inversion explains the religious landscape of post-Spellbreak Merata — why Daeva engagement has proliferated (they are the only powers that still answer), why it is more dangerous than it was (whatever once restrained them, the framework's resistance or the Avara's active opposition, is gone), and why faith in the Avara has become an act of loyalty to silence.

Daeva rarely manifest in physical form in Tessera — the framework, even damaged, prevents it except under extraordinary circumstances. More commonly they reach mortals through visions, possession, whispered voices, and the slow shaping of dreams. Each Daeva operates according to its own nature and desires. Some are subtle; some are overt; some barely seem aware of the mortals they influence.

Some Daeva believe they remember what they were before the Graveland — that they are true gods returned, ancient divinities whose names have simply been forgotten. For a few, this belief may have a grain of truth. Others have fabricated a divine identity from whatever fragments of self remain. Some Daeva refused to participate in creation when the Creators shaped Tessera — whether from pride, foresight, refusal to accept the obligations of creation, or simple indifference. Others are in the Graveland because their worlds failed and died, and they failed with them.

### The Great Daeva and Their Courts

The names and structure below follow the Nerebine Codex's chart: seven Great Daeva enthroned, each with lesser Daeva in its orbit, and one throne hollow. The chart is the best map mortals have of the Graveland. It is tidier than the territory.

**Leraye** — *The Perfect Act.* Precision, mastery, and the fracture that mastery produces. Its influence is felt in craftspeople who work until their hands bleed, in swordsmen who practice the same stroke ten thousand times, in rulers who reduce kingdoms to ruin in pursuit of an ideal that cannot exist. What Leraye perfects, it eventually breaks. In its orbit: **Iphareth** (perfection turned inward, upon the self — an unachievable ideal of purity, beauty, or form) and **Chayliel** (perfection of pursuit — the hunt that survives its own quarry, the hunger that arrives after victory and finds nothing satisfying in it). The old Alnathi teaching holds that the three were once a single being that perfected itself until it broke — which would make Leraye's court not a court but the orbiting shards of its own shattering. Iphareth and Chayliel each believe themselves the true original. Leraye has never addressed the question.

**Vaurun** — *The Revelation.* Knowledge that is true and costs everything. Vaurun does not lie; that is the ground of its danger, not a mitigation of it. The knowledge it provides is real. What it costs to receive, and what it does to a mind that has held it, is another matter. In its orbit: **Azdrath** (ambition, overreach — Vaurun's herald, the whisper that makes a mind ready to receive), and a third thing that no text names: a keeper of sealed knowledge, rendered in the Codex only as an empty cartouche. The darkest Spellbreak theory holds that it was Azdrath that carried the theft-of-divinity ritual to mortal ears — and scholars are damned to argue forever whether the herald acted on its principal's behalf or overreached, overreach being exactly its nature, and exactly what Vaurun would rely upon.

**Nymeris** — *The Veil.* Desire, misdirection, the beautiful untruth. The Daeva most likely to be mistaken for something benevolent — a patron spirit, a guardian, a fortune, a beautiful stranger. What it ultimately wants from any mortal transaction is unclear, which makes it more dangerous than a Daeva with obvious goals. In its orbit: **Orryn** (false memory, the rewritten past — in places where Orryn's influence takes root, history becomes unreliable and entire communities can be rewritten until they no longer know what they were). Vigil marginalia preserve a stranger claim: that Orryn originated in Vaurun's court and fled — the lie that escaped the truth. Whether Vaurun wants it back, the margin does not say.

**Mahadron** — *The Yoke.* Dominion, command, the will that admits no other. One of the most widely feared powers across all cultures, Mahadron does not deceive or whisper; it commands, and its influence grows in places of despotism and absolute control. Its court is the most rigid in the Graveland — among its lesser Daeva are **Vhastur, the Kneeling Voice** (the compulsion to obey that arrives dressed as one's own thought) and **Dravask the Surrendered** (the relief of yielding, the sweetness of the laid-down will). Nothing in Mahadron's orbit grows, because Mahadron permits no growth beneath it. The Named it has caught are the ones other Daeva pity — if Daeva pity.

**Selveth** — *The Hunger.* Appetite without terminus — a growing want that escalates until the host has consumed everything within reach and still cannot stop. Associated with hoarding, addiction, conquest that does not know when to end. In its orbit: **Velmis** (proliferation, plague, mindless spread — hunger's outward expression, consumption become contagion, manifesting most often at Spellbreak rifts where the boundary between the natural and the corrupted is already weak). Selveth's court is less a hierarchy than a digestion: lesser Daeva that fall into its orbit tend, over ages, to cease being distinguishable from it. More names have died in Selveth's orbit than in all the other courts combined.

**Karath** — *The End.* Inevitability, entropy, the reminder that all things stop. Karath does not destroy; it simply reminds things that they will end, and its influence is felt as a slow loosening of will, structure, and faith. Empires that begin to believe they cannot last are already hearing Karath. In its orbit: **Nuvaal** (erasure, silence — the ending of even memory; things simply stop, sounds fade, names become difficult to remember, and its presence is recognized only in retrospect, when something that should exist no longer does) and **Thessavane** (grief — the shadow the end casts forward). Thessavane is the anomaly of the Graveland: its divine memory is true. It alone among the catalogued powers was a genuine Creator, whose world failed and died, and its obsessive tending of mortal sorrow is not predation but projection — it does not create grief, only keeps what exists fresh, and consuming. It is particularly drawn to the Kynathi, whose grief is seven centuries deep and still fed by living memory, and it orbits Karath by choice: the only lesser Daeva in the Graveland that is where it is voluntarily, keeping company with the principle that took everything from it. Even Karath is careful with Nuvaal — erasure is the one thing inevitability cannot survive.

**Syrath** — *The Becoming.* Transformation that cannot be undone; the revelation of what one always was. Perhaps the most tolerated of the great Daeva among those who deal with such things carefully: Syrath offers genuine change, and does not corrupt so much as reveal. Its court is small and strange — lesser Daeva of thresholds, of molting, of the moment between shapes — and its orbit is the loosest of the seven, because Syrath alone among the great powers does not mind when things in its orbit change into something that leaves.

### The Hollow Throne

The eighth point of the Codex's sigil is drawn dark. An eighth Great Daeva existed — a power of **fire** — and is gone: consumed, burned out, or unmade so completely that even its erasure left no edges. No text records the dead name; whether this is discretion or genuine incapacity is argued, and the second possibility carries the fingerprints of Nuvaal.

The dead power's orbit persists, and two Named contest the throne. **Razael** is fire as *renewal* — overthrow, revolution, the burning away of corruption, the flame that clears dead growth so something new can live. Its cults are built on genuine grievance, and what Razael never mentions is that it does not discriminate between what needs to burn and what doesn't. **Phoros** is fire as *purification* — zeal, crusade, holy fire and divine mandate. Of all the Daeva it is the one most often openly worshipped as a true god, because it presents as one: its miracles are real, and its followers die sincerely believing they do sacred work, leaving ruins indistinguishable from those of true divine catastrophe. Each claims to be the Eighth returned. Each may sincerely believe it. Neither can prove it — possibly not even to itself. Their contest is the one open war in the Graveland, fought almost entirely through mortal proxies: Razael's revolutions against Phoros's crusades, grievance against righteousness, and coastlines of ash where their cults collide. Mortals caught between the two fires never learn that both issue from the same hollow seat.

A struck-through line in the Vigil Original is said to record a third claimant — one that had been mortal.

### Transcendence

If identity is the substance of Daevic power, then a mortal who assumes a Daevic name completely — the deeds, the nature, the cult, the story — manufactures exactly the material the Graveland runs on. Where a name is contested, vacant, or held by something in decline, the path exists: the aspirant does not worship the Daeva but *performs* it, in the world, until the name has more living reality in the mortal than in the Graveland. Death, or a ritual crossing, completes the accretion.

Whether the mortal becomes the Daeva, or the name merely acquires a new mask, is indistinguishable from outside — and possibly from inside. The Vigil of Nerebyasca is believed to keep the only serious scholarship on the question, in a chapter of the Codex that no copy contains. In Kynathi theology, transcendence is the supreme abomination — the Inverted Return: severance *chosen*, a soul trading its homecoming along the weave for a throne among dead things.

### Beyond the Chart

The Codex's schema does not survive contact with the field. Tindaire's anomaly records include lesser Daeva that answer coherently to two orbits at once, entities matching no charted power that nonetheless display great-Daeva coherence, and Blightland-edge communities whose lived demonology maps to no eight-pointed anything. The honest position, held privately even at Nerebyasca, is that the sigil is a chart of the shore drawn from a ship in fog — and that the fog is not incidental, but the subject's medium.

### A Note on Sources

Nearly everything above reaches the present day through elven hands, and this should be read as a fact about the record, not about the truth. The elven peoples were long-lived, literate, institutionally obsessive, and standing at or near the center of the catastrophe; of course they dominate the surviving scholarship. The very words *Avara* and *Daeva* are loanwords — elven terms that conquered Meratan learning through Tindaire the way a scholarly tongue conquers a continent, by being the language the libraries were written in. Vocabulary is not ownership. The powers themselves predate and exceed every living people, and the elven schism is the origin of two elven *responses* to them — not of the powers, and not of contact, which has always been available to anyone near a thin place, on any coast, in any age.

Nor are the elves the only keepers, or the oldest, or necessarily the closest. The Vahari faced whatever mortals and the divine were to each other before any living people existed, and the Sehrati priestly caste guards chambers that owe nothing to Nerebyasca. The Reshai practice of spirits and masks engages something older and less framework-bound than any elven method — a working relationship with the substance of identity itself, held as craft rather than theory. The Myrathi Reading answers, which no elven tradition can claim. Human traditions remain the least recorded and the most consequential: in an economy where sustained mortal attention is power, the largest gravity acting on the Graveland is not the Codex's thousands of readers but humanity's millions of believers — the folk religion, hedge-practice, and fireside fear of the continent's dominant people, almost none of it yet written down. The Codex and the Doctrine of the Severed are the loudest voices in the record. They should not be mistaken for the deepest.

## The Elder Races (Urath)

Scholars and oral tradition across multiple cultures reference an ancient people who predated the current division of races. The academic term, reconstructed from linguistic fragments at Tindaire, is **Elathi** — meaning roughly "the first people." In older oral traditions, particularly among the Dyrathi, the name **Urath** survives. What can be recovered about them is fragmentary and contested.

The Kynathi claim direct divine descent from the Urath, positioning their own people as the truest inheritors of Tessera's original civilized lineage. In Kynathi theology, the Urath were not mortal in the way current peoples are mortal — they were the first divine inhabitants of the world, and the Kynathi's magical gifts are evidence of this inheritance. This claim is not accepted by all peoples.

What is more broadly accepted is that the Kynathi, Alnathi, Myrathi, and Dyrathi share some ancient common ancestry, reflected in the *-athi* suffix that appears across all their names. Whether this reflects a literal shared lineage or is a linguistic artifact of long cohabitation is debated. The Dyrathi are divided on it: some clans accept the elder-races concept and embrace kinship with the elven peoples; others reject the connection entirely; a few hold their own divergent tradition in which the Urath were a subterranean people whose surface descendants eventually became the elven races, not the other way around. The extinct Vorathi — the flesh-shapers credited with the making of the orcish and goblinoid peoples — complicate the question further: if they were a true elven branch, then the made peoples stand in some relation to the *-athi* family that no living elven tradition is eager to define.

## Races

### Kynathi (High Elves)
Before the Spellbreak the Kynathi were the continental power: the **Kynathi Hegemony**, a civilization of extraordinary craft, learning, and ambition whose reach — direct, or through client peoples — covered much of Merata. Illuvinel, Arhebel, and Isrannaes are its bones. The catastrophe gutted it within a generation: the framework its high magic depended upon cracked, its god fell silent, and its client peoples — humans chief among them — went their own ways. What remains is the **Natari Dominion** (see Geopolitics): diminished, not weak. Long-lived and slow to breed — a Kynathi may see nine centuries — they have never recovered their numbers, and never will on any timescale that matters to their rivals.

The length of Kynathi life gives the present age its particular cruelty: the eldest among them personally remember the world before. They heard Aevorn answer prayer; they walked Illuvinel when its waterfalls ran. These witnesses are dying now, one by one — and in a world where memory is the substance of power, Natari understands precisely what it is losing. The Dominion is not a museum. It is a hospice of witnesses, and its edicts are enforced by people who remember why they were written. What the witnesses remember is not innocent: the golden age they carry is gilded — by grief, by pride, and by the ordinary way the old burnish their youth — and some of them know it, and gild it anyway.

They venerate the Avara, a pantheon of ancestor-gods, and believe themselves descended from the Urath, the divine first people of Tessera. Kynathi theology frames the Spellbreak as a wound to the divine order — and the silence of their chief god, Aevorn, as its deepest consequence.

Kynathi orthodoxy remembers the ancestors of the Alnathi as the **Anchorite heresy**: mystics who claimed a sight past the weave's edge, preached that creation itself must end, and were cast out for it in the age when the Avara still answered. That the catastrophe the heretics prophesied then arrived is a fact Kynathi theology has never comfortably metabolized. Orthodoxy resolves it the only way it can: the "foresight" was a Daevic whisper, and the whisperers helped make it true.

Some Kynathi view the Alnathi as aberrant, degenerate, or traitors to the elven heritage — from a Kynathi theological standpoint, Daeva worship is not merely heresy but a choice to bind oneself to dead things rather than seek the divine return. Formal Kynathi demonology — the **Doctrine of the Severed** — inverts the Alnathi approach entirely: it holds the Daeva to be dead limbs cut from creation, without true hierarchy or individuality, and teaches that every name fixed to them feeds them. The doctrine forbids naming, cataloguing, or studying the Daeva; at Natari it has the force of law, and the burden of forbidden knowledge is carried by a small sequestered order, the Wardens of the Unspoken.

### Alnathi (Dark Elves)
The Alnathi and the Kynathi are one people twice divided — first by heresy, then by catastrophe — and the schism was about the Daeva from the beginning.

Millennia before the Spellbreak, in the age when the Avara still answered, a current arose within elven orthodoxy: mystics of Luneth's and Morindel's cults — the hidden and the threshold — who turned their contemplation past the weave's edge and attained what their descendants call the **Grave-Sight**: a direct apprehension of the Graveland and of what it is. What they saw was a shore of dead creations — failed worlds and fallen makers — and they drew the inference orthodoxy could not tolerate: that this happens every time. Creation is not permanent; worlds are made, run their course, and end; the Graveland is not an aberration but a destination; and the Avara's promise of the return is a promise made inside a system that itself ends. Whether the seers were expelled or walked out of a temple that had become a cage is remembered differently on each side. That they went willingly, in the end, is disputed by no one. Tradition preserves no founder's name; the Alnathi hold that the first seer gave the name away, and do not say to what.

From the Grave-Sight the movement's schools diverged, and both survive as substrate in Alnathi tradition. The **Harbored** taught the Daeva-bond as refuge: a soul bound to the Graveland persists as the Daeva persist, and will not dissolve with creation when the end comes. The **Moorbound** taught something more radical — that souls bound to the Daeva anchor those powers to the living world, and in anchoring them, anchor the world itself: a mooring against the cycle's turning, prolonging creation in defiance of its makers' design. On the Moorbound reading, Daeva practice is not self-damnation but sacrifice — the practitioner spends their own return to keep the world alive — and the Avara's ancient hostility to the practice was never protection, but enforcement of a demolition schedule. No living Alnathi institution asserts either doctrine plainly. They persist beneath the surface: in funerary practice, in the Vigil's charge at Nerebyasca, in the deliberateness with which the Alnathi engage powers that other peoples flee. Outsiders call the movement, and the people it became, the **Anchorite heresy**; the name is Natari's, and the Alnathi long ago stopped objecting to it.

The exiles settled the continental interior — the region that became the Blightlands — and were at or near the center of the Spellbreak. Within a few generations of the catastrophe the transformation set in: skin turned gray, blue-tinged, deep purple. The cause is genuinely undecidable. Spellbreak exposure and generations of deliberate Daevic practice were operating on the same population at the same moment. The Kynathi accusation — that the change is Syrath's mark, the wage of the old heresy made flesh — is plausible; so is the Alnathi mainstream position that it is a Spellbreak scar, kin to the Sahridi sands. A few Alnathi mystics embrace the Syrath reading as an honor rather than a slander: what one becomes is what one always was. The truth is unrecoverable — and the Edict of Unnaming makes it doctrinally illegal for Natari ever to substantiate its own accusation.

Daeva worship is common among the Alnathi and is not considered evil in traditional Alnathi culture; it is approached with caution and deliberate practice, formalized over the post-Spellbreak centuries from what had been generations of forbidden discipline. Their favored Daeva — Leraye, Nymeris, Chayliel, Syrath — reflect a culture that values precision, adaptability, the capacity for self-transformation, and navigating the edges of power. The Daeva they avoid — Mahadron, Selveth, Nuvaal, Razael, Vaurun — are those that threaten or erase the self, rather than sharpen it. The Alnathi are not reckless in what they engage with; they are deliberate. The death-priests of Nerebyasca — the order known as **the Vigil** — compiled and keep the Nerebine Codex, the foundational mortal chart of the Graveland; the order predates the Codex by an unknown span, and may be as old as the exile itself.

The Alnathi survived the Spellbreak and its aftermath but tend not to expand territorially or assert dominance. They often find it difficult to integrate into societies where Kynathi-influenced prejudice is common. Some Alnathi clans have developed their own forms of racial superiority in response. Their homeland sits in rugged interior terrain adjacent to the Blightlands — territory other peoples cannot hold, and the only country the catastrophe left that is indisputably theirs.

For all the mutual hatred, the two elven peoples remain one temperament pointed in opposite directions: long-lived, deliberate, tradition-bound, organized around their dead, each maintaining a sequestered order that carries dangerous knowledge — the Wardens who refuse to write, the Vigil who control the writing. The Kynathi answered the Spellbreak with discipline aimed at restoring what was; the Alnathi with discipline aimed at mastering what is. Scholars at Tindaire note the symmetry constantly. Both peoples find the observation intolerable.

### Myrathi (Sea Elves)
The most visually and culturally distinct of the elven peoples. The Myrathi occupy the Shattered Isles in the Orathian Sea, and their impossibly fast warships are feared by all sailors — a Myrathi sail on the horizon is read as a death omen. The fear is earned but the reading is wrong, because there is no pattern for outsiders to read.

Myrathi culture turns on **the encounter**. Any meeting — a foreign vessel, a whale-run, a storm-wrack, a stranger cast ashore — must be acted upon as circumstance dictates, and circumstance is read in the moment by the ship's **tidecaller**: the winds, the water, the taste of salt and brine, the position of the stars or sun. The practice is called **the Reading**, and its verdict is absolute. The Myrathi do not raid, kill, pillage, or take captives for sport, plunder, or even survival; they do what the sea dictated. This is why no outsider ever learns why a Myrathi vessel killed all but one of a crew and carried him away, or disabled a ship and left it drifting, or drew alongside, did nothing, and departed. Someone taken by a Reading was not enslaved but *named by the sea* — some are returned years later, changed; some become Myrathi; some are delivered to a place and left there, for reasons no one, possibly including the Myrathi, will ever explain.

Whether something truly answers the Reading — the sea itself, something beneath it, something the Myrathi would not call a Daeva, or nothing at all — is unknown to outsiders and perhaps contested among the Myrathi themselves. Sailors of every nation keep countermeasures — offerings thrown overboard, silence on deck, hull markings — none of which verifiably work, which is worse than if they did. Seradon's admiralty maintains a corpus of encounter accounts; generations of study have found that the only variable predicting outcomes is conditions at the moment of contact, which is exactly what the Myrathi have been saying all along.

### Dyrathi (Dwarves)
The Dyrathi are ancestrally linked to the other *-athi* races through the ancient Urath lineage. Unlike the Kynathi and Giants, they are not in obvious decline — if anything, the opposite: sheltered underground from a substantially surface catastrophe, the Dyrathi came through the Spellbreak the least damaged of the great peoples, and seven centuries of careful solvency have made their holds the quiet creditors and toll-keepers of everyone else's recovery. They maintain substantial mountain holds, deep mines, and underground cities along the mountain range forming the northern edge of Merata's human heartland. Their holds control the key passes between the human kingdoms to the south and the wilder north — a geographic leverage that has given them a pragmatic importance that sheer population numbers do not reflect.

Some Dyrathi holdings are integrated into Meratan geopolitics through trade and treaty; others are entirely self-contained, hostile to outsiders, and indifferent to whatever happens on the surface. Fully integrated Dyrathi living among human or other cultures are not uncommon. The Dyrathi are renowned craftspeople — their forges produce the finest weapons and armor in Merata.

### Humans
Diverse, adaptable, and the continent's plurality: the most numerous people of Merata, and the fastest to recover from the catastrophe — but masters of nothing. Before the Spellbreak most human polities existed in the Kynathi Hegemony's shadow as clients, tributaries, and auxiliaries — but they had not begun that way. There were human kingdoms before the Hegemony absorbed them, conquered or coerced into clientage in the deep past, and the memory of having once been free ran under the client centuries like groundwater. The catastrophe freed them into a broken world, and they filled the vacuum faster than any other people could. The Merathine Empire — humanity's premature bid for the hegemony the Kynathi lost — has been receding for three centuries, but its legacy shapes Meratan politics more than any living institution. Humans exist in every climate and culture, from the tundra peoples of Isenfall to the highland clans of Teutania to the trading cities of the south, and the memory of client status colors human-Kynathi relations to this day — deference in some courts, and in others a quiet hunger to see the Dominion finished. Human religious life — the folk Courtesies, the imperial Kingsblood and Pactorate, and the answer-seeking movements grown since the catastrophe — is treated in a companion document.

The people of Isenfall are culturally distinct from southern humans: their ancestors crossed from the northern continent of Arlengh generations before the Spellbreak, and when the catastrophe sealed the northern ocean in storm and ice, the crossing became one-way and the homeland became a memory. Indigenous tribes that predated the Arlengh settlers have dwindled significantly.

### Giants
A race in decline — and the decline predates the catastrophe. In the deep past the Giants fought the **Giant War** against the Kynathi Hegemony, and it broke their strength generations before the Spellbreak ever came; what the war was over, and who could truly be said to have won it, survives only in fragments, and the Giants do not discuss it with outsiders. The Giants of the present mostly keep to their own, avoiding more prolific peoples except when defending territory. Some communities require tribute or offering from nearby settlements depending on longstanding local relationships. Their fuller history is largely lost — including whether they share stock with the giants of Arlengh, whose orphaned descendants still sail the world's oceans.

### Sea Giants
The Sea Giants are Arlengh's orphans. Their people originated on the northern continent, and when the Spellbreak sealed it in permanent storm and ice, those aboard the great hulks — fleets, colonies, or something else entirely — were left with nowhere to return. They have no land-based civilization: they live entirely aboard three known **sea-hulks**, vessels so vast they are mistaken for islands at distance, and some phantom islands on old charts are almost certainly hulk sightings no one understood. What happened on Arlengh — whether its giants and its human peoples destroyed one another, whether the ice took everyone, whether something worse occurred — is unknown and, with the crossing sealed, likely unknowable. Whether the hulks' endless circling is exile, mourning, or search has never been answered by anyone qualified to know.

An encounter is a generational event, less a raid than a natural disaster — the hurricane that comes once in a hundred years. Port cities keep hulk protocols that have never been tested in living memory, and the signs are remembered the way storms are: harbor birds going quiet, the swell turning wrong, and then a silhouette on the horizon that the lookout logs as an uncharted island — until it moves. When a hulk makes landfall it strips a city the way a whaler strips a whale — timber, metal, livestock, stores, and people — and departs without message, negotiation, or apparent malice. The reported "alliance" with the Myrathi is a misreading: Myrathi vessels are occasionally seen moving ahead of a hulk, heralds by omen rather than treaty — their Readings, not any compact, put them there.

### Reshai
A monkey-like people indigenous to the Pritani Jungle of southeastern Merata. Mostly keeping to themselves, the Reshai practice an animistic spiritual tradition centered on the veneration of nature spirits and their divine patron, **Jakanu** — a trickster deity associated with cleverness, martial wit, and the divine nature of the Reshai people. Masks are ritually significant in Reshai culture, worn for different spiritual and social purposes. Their martial tradition, practiced primarily with the staff, is associated with Jakanu's own legendary feats.

### Sehrati
The people of the Sahridi Desert — reclusive, mysterious, and rarely encountered beyond the desert's edges. They appear broadly human in form, with deep amber to dark brown skin, dark hair, and a notable tendency toward pale gold or amber eyes. Their dress is elaborate desert attire ornamented with patterns that echo the carvings found in the ruins they consider sacred; ritual scarification is common.

The Sahridi Desert was not always a desert. The Spellbreak transformed the region, burying the remains of the **Vahari** — an extinct civilization that predates all currently living peoples — beneath the sand. The ruins that protrude above the surface are treated by the Sehrati as sacred ground, approached with ritual and reverence. A priestly caste holds knowledge of deeper buried ruins: some chambers sealed since before memory, some entered and not spoken of again. The Sehrati do not know they may be the distant descendants of the Vahari. The connection has been entirely lost.

### The Made Peoples (Orcs, Goblins, Trolls, Ogres, and Kin)
The peoples that scholarship calls *made* are, by the most widely credited account, the surviving works of the **Vorathi**: a lost branch of the elven family — flesh-shapers who bred and built servitor peoples for labor and war in the deep pre-Spellbreak past. The Vorathi are extinct, and were extinct before the catastrophe. How they died is contested in the way everything important in Tessera is contested: some accounts say their creations rose against them; some say the other elven peoples destroyed them for practicing abomination; some say they made, or reached for, something that unmade them. Natari does not discuss the chapter at all — the Hegemony's records of the Vorathi, if they exist, have never been produced — and the silence is generally read as an answer of some kind.

Their former servitors outlived them, and in the vacuum after the Spellbreak proliferated across Merata's margins — the hills, the wastes, the eastern grasslands, the edges of every map the kingdoms draw. The made peoples' own traditions about their origin vary profoundly: some curse the makers by name, some venerate the freeing itself as the founding event, and some deny having been made at all, holding themselves peoples in their own right whose histories owe the Vorathi nothing. Their existence complicates the Urath ancestry debate in ways elven scholarship prefers not to examine — if the makers were elves, the made are, in some terrible sense, family. Legal and social standing varies enormously by kingdom; among the clans of the Vezhan Steppe, orcs and goblinoids ride as equals. The individual cultures of these peoples, and their individual relationships to the making, remain to be developed.

### The Riftborn
The youngest people in Merata — younger than the kingdoms, younger than most ruins. In the decades after the Spellbreak, children began to be born changed in communities near rifts, leyline fractures, and the Blightlands' edge: horns, tails, claws, eyes that see in darkness, skin and hair in colors nature never issued. The changes bred true. Seven centuries on, the riftborn are a people — thin-spread, mostly urban or frontier, common in Loneridge and the Blightland-facing settlements, rare and remarked-upon everywhere else.

They are the catastrophe made flesh, and are treated accordingly: blamed in bad years, scapegoated after incursions, barred from some cities and merely watched in others. The theological question that follows them is genuinely unsettled — whether they are Aberration-touched, a new people entire, or something the framework produced *on purpose* in its breaking. The riftborn themselves are not one culture and hold no single answer; "riftborn" is the common tongue's word, and the largest self-identified communities use names of their own. To be developed further.

### Vampires
In Tessera's metaphysics, life is participation in the weave, and blood is its living medium — the current through which a body carries its share of creation: vitality, selfhood, the continuity that makes a person the same person across their days. A vampire is a being whose own participation is broken — not severed cleanly, as death severs, but ruined and left open — and which persists by drinking the participation of others. What a vampire takes with blood is not nourishment but *being*: the victim survives diminished, memories thinned, their name a little harder to hold; heavy or repeated feeding can erase a person without ever killing them.

The blood must be warm and living — dead blood carries no current — and the need is perpetual, because a vampire does not feed to survive. A vampire is past surviving. It feeds to remain *itself*. Starved, a vampire does not die; it dissolves into un-being: memory goes first, then the name, then the shape of the self, until what remains is a hollowed thing of pure appetite — mindless, close kin to the Murmur, and far more dangerous to ordinary people than any deliberate lord of the night. Elder vampires fear the stake far less than they fear forgetting. And because blood carries memory, old blood is rich: what a vampire drinks from the long-lived it keeps in fragments — which makes the dying witnesses of Natari the most coveted vessels in the world. The Dominion guards its elders against more than time.

What made the first vampires is unknown — and the **First Night**, long told as their origin, is better understood as their greatest event. When the framework shattered, everyone then within the threshold of death — the world's dying, caught mid-crossing in the catastrophe's instant — was sundered half in and half out, unable to finish dying, unable to resume living. Across Merata, the deathbeds of that hour rose: a mass generation of every people, all of an age with the catastrophe, all remembering the world before — a second population of witnesses, undying where Natari's are dying, hidden where Natari's are guarded. But vampires existed before the breaking. The eldest are older than the catastrophe, their own origin lost even to themselves — starved and re-fed across the ages until the memory of what made them dissolved — and they regard the First Night generation the way old blood regards an infestation: too many, too young, made all at once by an accident rather than by whatever made *them*. Rival accounts of the deep origin credit a Daevic mark — hunger without terminus made flesh — or something older still; the truth may no longer exist anywhere, in any mind. Transmission is less disputed: a vampire's blood, given to the dying, reenacts the broken crossing in a single body — the First Night in miniature, and perhaps, some scholars note uneasily, a reenactment of something the First Night was itself reenacting. Kynathi doctrine classes the vampire as walking severance and kills it on sight; the Vigil's classification struggles, for the vampire is neither Eldritch nor Aberration but some third thing, and the struggle is recorded in the Codex's margins.

### Were-Creatures
Were-creatures exist across Tessera and are associated above all with Gloamwood, where the condition — if condition is the word — appears to breed true. Alnathi tradition connects them to Syrath's touch upon the world: completed transformations, the Becoming made lineage. The Codex raises the question and pointedly declines to settle it. To be developed further.

## Geopolitics## Geopolitics

What follows charts the Merathine sphere — the imperial successor landscape of human Merata — together with the powers that press upon it. It is a sampling, not a census: the continent holds more polities, peoples, and arrangements than any single account, and the map here is explicitly incomplete. The internal politics of the non-human powers — the Dominion's factions, the holds' councils, the Isles, the steppe — await their own treatments.

### The Natari Dominion

The Kynathi rump state — the direct continuation, in law and in self-understanding, of the pre-Spellbreak Kynathi Hegemony that once covered much of Merata. The Dominion holds Natari and a contracted crescent of coastal and hinterland territory, governs it under the old edicts, and has spent seven centuries in the condition of an empire that refuses to finish falling: diminished, encircled by successor peoples, periodically written off, and still standing. It is past its capacity for conquest and nowhere near past its capacity for harm — Dominion arts, Dominion knowledge, and Dominion gold move through Meratan politics far beyond Dominion borders, and its diplomacy is patient, contemptuous, and effective. Human courts that deal with Natari come away feeling they have negotiated with something that regards the entire post-Spellbreak order as a squatters' arrangement it has not yet troubled to contest. The Dominion's true strategic problem is not any rival but arithmetic: the witnesses are dying, the birth-houses are quiet, and every policy Natari pursues is downstream of a question it never speaks aloud — what a restoration is *for*, if no one is left to restore.

### The Merathine Empire

Humanity's bid for the hegemony the Kynathi lost — made, in hindsight, about three centuries too early. Founded roughly a century after the Spellbreak, when the first Merathine united the delta city of Cassidor with the river kingdoms of the Maren under a single crown, the empire expanded through the disordered generations that followed until, at its peak, it governed more of Merata than any human polity before or since. The founding was framed then, and is taught still, as a *restoration* — not a new crown but the return of human kingship after the long clientage, with the first Merathine's blood-claim reaching back, by genealogies of contested honesty, to the kings the Hegemony had unmade. The claim's truth matters less than its work: it makes the empire the heir of something older than the Kynathi conquest, and human rule in Merata a resumption rather than an invention. Whether it named itself for the continent, or for its founder, or the continent for it, is a question Tindaire's antiquarians genuinely cannot settle; the crown has always preferred the ambiguity, which asserts universal rule either way.

It has been receding for some three hundred years. A formal imperial structure still exists — an emperor of the Merathine line still sits in Cassidor — but the empire now controls perhaps a third of what it once did. Its armies cannot enforce edicts far from the heartland; its legal institutions and cultural influence persist across successor kingdoms even where its military does not. The dynasty's blood-claim is not merely ceremonial in Meratan eyes: in a world where blood carries the current of participation and identity, an unbroken founding line is believed to *hold* something, and rival court factions in Lorindale and Seradon back rival branches of the family accordingly. The empire survives partly through inertia, partly through ceremony, and partly because no single successor state has been strong enough to formally dissolve it. Cassidor itself is grand but hollowed — its palaces and institutions still stand, its population has declined, and it runs on Maren delta trade wealth and the accumulated prestige of its past. The founding itself — who the first Merathine was, and by what mandate the crown was claimed — is wrapped in the **Kingsblood**, the imperial cult of the restored royal line, treated with the rest of human religion in a companion document.

### The Maren River

The primary river system of Merata — a branching network draining much of the continent's interior, fanning into a fertile delta at Cassidor. It is simultaneously a highway and a border, a source of agricultural wealth and political contestation. The Merathine Empire was built around control of the Maren and its tributaries; the empire's fading has made the river's management a persistent source of inter-kingdom tension.

### The Successor Landscape

The empire's recession left behind not four neat kingdoms but a heterogeneous landscape — successor crowns, chartered leagues, march-lords, free cities, and peripheries that were never anyone's subjects. The powers below are the largest pieces, not the whole board.

**Lorindale** sits in the fertile heartland south of the Maren — the granary that believes it is the heir. The most pro-imperial of the successor states, it treats the empire less as a sovereign than as an inheritance to be managed into its own hands: Lorindale gold endows imperial offices, Lorindale jurists staff the imperial courts, and Lorindale backs its favored branch of the Merathine line with a patience that assumes the succession is a matter of time. Its expansionist interests are the best funded in Merata, organized through chartered land companies whose paper promises estates in the Dyrathi approaches and the Gloamwood margins — charters that sell briskly in Cassidor whether or not anyone survives to claim the land. Lorindale's weakness is the mirror of its strength: everything it is depends on the river, on the imperial law that governs the river, and on the assumption that the game of inheritance will be decided in courtrooms rather than fields.

**Seradon** holds the southern coast — pragmatic, maritime, and quietly the most worldly of the kingdoms, because it is the one that lives with the sea and everything the sea contains. Its admiralty maintains the continent's only serious archive of Myrathi encounters and tolerates the coastal *quiet-water customs* — offerings, silences, hull-marks — that official doctrine dismisses as superstition and every Seradine captain observes anyway. Seradon is also the kingdom that actually deals with the Natari Dominion: Dominion trade moves through Seradine ports under agreements neither party publicizes, and Seradon's court has learned what Lorindale's has not — what it costs, and what it earns, to negotiate with something older than your civilization. Its conviction is that the coast can survive the empire's death, and its policy is to be positioned for the day.

The Lorindale–Seradon rivalry is not temperamental but historical: two generations ago the kingdoms fought the **Salt War** over the Maren mouths, and the **Treaty of the Mouths** that ended it — brokered by the crown, satisfying no one — divided the delta tolls by a formula each side regards as robbery. Every court intrigue between them since is that treaty, continued by other means.

**The Maren League** is not a kingdom at all: a chain of chartered merchant cities along the river's middle course, created by imperial charter in the empire's building age to move grain and man the locks, and now, in the fading, effectively self-governing. The League's freedom rests entirely on imperial paper — its charters are grants from the crown, valid precisely as long as a crown exists to have granted them — which makes the League the strangest faction in imperial politics: cities that despise the emperor and need the empire, paper loyalists who would bankroll the throne's defense and celebrate its humiliation in the same season. In any succession crisis the League will back whichever claimant confirms the charters; it says so openly, and calls it honesty.

**Vrasar** is the northeastern successor state — rugged, cold, martial, and now the continent's eastern shield. The Banner-Sworn's probing raids have put the kingdom on a war footing: the marches are fortifying, refugees drift west, and Vrasar hires made-peoples mercenaries to fight made-peoples riders, an irony the kingdom has stopped finding remarkable. Its relationship with the Dyrathi holds along its northern border has deepened into dependence — Dyrathi steel, Dyrathi credit, Dyrathi tolls waived against future considerations — and the holds now own more of Vrasar's war than either party advertises. Vrasar maintains formal imperial membership and open contempt for it; its calls for aid go unanswered, and the kingdom increasingly behaves like what it may soon declare itself to be. Whether the horde makes Vrasar the empire's grave or its refounding is the openest question in Meratan politics.

**Juna** is the smallest of the principal crowns and the strangest: a northern kingdom wedged between the Dyrathi mountains and the Isenfall tundra, whose real wealth is a dead city. Juna controls the approaches to Illuvinel, the most magnificent ruin of the Kynathi golden age, and has built its treasury on wardenship of the corpse — licensing expeditions, taxing recoveries, and hanging unlicensed diggers with a rigor the Merathine office at Loneridge can only envy. The trade has made Juna rich beyond its size and placed it in permanent, carefully managed conflict with the Natari Dominion, which regards Illuvinel as sacred bones and Juna's licenses as the sale of desecration. Juna's court has become unexpectedly expert in Dominion diplomacy, Isenfall custom, and the practical theology of what may and may not be safely removed from a dead elven city — knowledge it did not want and cannot now afford to lose.

Beyond and between the named powers lies the rest of the landscape: march-lords along every frontier whose crown allegiance is a courtesy and whose real policy is made with whoever lives across the border; free cities holding old immunities; Pactorate vowhouse estates, licensed shrines, and pilgrimage towns whose sacredness is inseparable from imperial paper; and the never-imperial peripheries — Teutania, Isenfall, and others unnamed — which were no one's successors because they were never anyone's subjects.

### The Political Divide

Meratan pamphleteers describe a continent divided between two parties: those who would push the human frontier outward into non-human lands, and those who would hold it and trade across it. The description is a simplification that everyone uses and everyone knows to be one. The political weather is real; the tidy division is the pamphleteers'.

What actually exists is a scatter of local, material conflicts over which the frame gets draped. The **Weir War**, fought on one of the Maren's eastern tributaries over water-rights and mill-weirs between a Lorindale march and a League city, killed several hundred people before anyone called it ideological. The **Greenmarch collapse** — a chartered land company whose paper promised estates in the Gloamwood margins and whose surveyors never returned — ruined investors in two kingdoms and made *charter man* a slur in both. The **Harrow Compact**, a border lord's private treaty of tolls and mutual defense with a Dyrathi hold, was formally disavowed by his crown and is quietly honored by it, because the alternative is a war the crown cannot fund. And the riot at **Vellin**, where a rift incursion was answered with a pogrom against the town's riftborn quarter, is the kind of event both tendencies claim as proof of their case. From incidents like these the pamphlet-frame is assembled; *charter men* and *treaty men* are what the tendencies call each other, and almost no one calls themselves. Non-human peoples, for their part, cannot simply ally with a friendly kingdom — they must navigate the actual scatter, court by court and march by march.

Over all of it sits the **imperial question**. The Merathine line runs thin — the emperor is old, the direct succession uncertain — and in a world where blood is believed to hold something, the inheritance of the founding blood is not academic. Lorindale and Seradon back rival branches; the Maren League backs the charters and says so; Vrasar asks why it bleeds for a crown that sends letters; and a quiet position whispered everywhere holds that the empire should be allowed to finish dying so that something real can be built from the estate. The Banner-Sworn have scrambled every calculation at once, because an external threat strengthens every argument — conquest, alliance, unity, dissolution — and nothing clarifies a continent's politics like a horde.

### The Vezhan Steppe and the Banner-Sworn

East of Vrasar's marches, beyond the last imperial milestone, the grasslands run to the horizon: the Vezhan Steppe, home for centuries to horse clans both human and made — orcish, goblin, and mixed-blood peoples who drifted east after their makers' fall and learned the steppe's economy of motion. The clans raided one another and the frontier in the ordinary way of steppes until, within living memory, they stopped raiding one another. A single Khal now commands what the clans call the **Banner-Sworn** — a confederation of unprecedented size, bound by oaths sworn in blood, which in Tessera is not a figure of speech. The Banner-Sworn have taken the eastern marches' measure in probing raids for a decade; Vrasar arms; Juna watches; the empire sends letters. What the Khal intends — conquest, tribute, a homeland, or something stranger — is unknown, and the rumor that something rides with the horde, whispering the hunt onward, is exactly the kind of rumor Merata's frontiers always produce and can never confirm. The Banner-Sworn are the one unambiguously rising power on a declining continent, and every court in Merata has begun to reckon with what that means.

## A Brief Chronology

Dates are reckoned from the Spellbreak (PS). All are approximate; the record is thin, contested, and in places deliberately damaged.

**Deep past** — The age of the Kynathi Hegemony, spanning millennia. The Anchorite schism and the Alnathi exile into the interior. The old human kingdoms are conquered or coerced into clientage. The Giant War breaks the Giants' strength. The Vorathi flesh-shapers rise and are destroyed — generations before the catastrophe, by causes contested. The Shattering of the Isles, after which the sea begins to speak. Settlers cross from Arlengh to Isenfall.

**The last age — the Waning.** For a century or more before the breaking, the world runs down: the Avara answer less and less, magic costs more, births thin among the long-lived. The Hegemony's final age is magnificent and frightened. Isrannaes is carved in haste. Whatever desperate remedies were attempted at the end — and by whom — is the substance of every Spellbreak theory.

**0 PS — The Spellbreak.** The framework shatters. Arlengh seals behind storm and ice. The Blightlands and the Sahridi Desert are made. The Alnathi transformation begins. Aevorn and the Avara fall silent. The First Night: across Merata, the deathbeds of the hour rise — the greatest event in vampire history, though not, the eldest insist, its beginning.

**c. 0–100 PS — The Sundering Years.** The Hegemony collapses to the Natari Dominion. The client peoples scatter into independence. Riftborn children begin to be born near the wounds. Vaelthir the Unspeaking restores the Unnaming at Natari.

**c. 100 PS** — The first Merathine unites Cassidor and the river kingdoms; the Merathine Empire is founded.

**c. 200–250 PS** — Ashvenn the Twice-Grieved redacts the Nerebine Codex at Nerebyasca.

**c. 250–450 PS — The imperial peak.** The empire's greatest extent. Tindaire is founded under imperial patronage.

**c. 450 PS–present — The fading.** Imperial recession; the successor kingdoms rise into the slack. The Lethengarde Incident empties the observatory city. The Loneridge rift opens and is contained by the Rift-Breakers within living memory. The Vezhan clans unite under a single Khal.

**750 PS — The present.** The eldest Kynathi witnesses of the world before are dying. The Banner-Sworn gather in the east. The empire recedes; the Dominion endures; the rifts remain.

## The Avara

The Avara are the pantheon of ancestor-gods venerated by the Kynathi. They have names, spheres of influence, and defined personalities. Kynathi belief holds that the Avara were the divine first inhabitants of Tessera — that the Kynathi are their mortal-born descendants, and that death is a return to the divine realm rather than an ending. Whether the Avara are the canonical gods of the world, or simply the Kynathi cultural interpretation of a more universal divine truth, is unknown. Other cultures across Merata hold their own spiritual traditions, which may or may not reflect parallel interpretations of the same beings.

Before the Spellbreak, the Avara were present. Kynathi liturgy and the fragments preserved at Tindaire describe a living orthodoxy: gods who answered prayer, guided their peoples, on rare occasions walked among them — and actively opposed the far powers that pressed against the weave from outside. That presence did not end at the catastrophe; it faded before it. Through the Waning the Avara answered less and less, and their silence since the Spellbreak is, on one reading, not a wound at all — merely the completion of a withdrawal already long underway. Kynathi orthodoxy rejects the reading with a vigor that measures its fear of it. The Avara are largely silent since the Spellbreak. They cannot manifest physically, do not perform miracles, and prayer is an act of faith rather than reliable communication. On extremely rare occasions, an Avara may speak and act through a mortal host — typically someone of extraordinary devotion — usually at the cost of that person's life. Shrines, artifacts, and ruins retaining some bound essence of the divine are highly sought by priests, zealots, adventurers, and relic hunters. Some heretics seek to destroy them: from ideology, from Daevic influence, from iconoclasm, or from a desire to drain the residual power for themselves.

What the silence *is* remains the deepest question in Kynathi theology. Orthodoxy holds it to be a wound: the Spellbreak severed the connection, and restoration is possible — the hope around which Natari's entire civilization is organized. The old Anchorite reading, anathema at Natari, holds it to be a departure: the Avara are not silenced but withdrawing, as makers do when a world's course is run. Nothing in seven centuries has settled the question, and the two readings describe two different worlds — one wounded and waiting, one ending on schedule.

**Aevorn** — *The Radiant; Creation & Magic.* Chief of the Avaric pantheon. The god of the divine creative act, whose bloodline the Kynathi believe flows through them. Aevorn's silence since the Spellbreak is the deepest wound in Kynathi civilization — the central theological crisis around which Natari's edicts, their hostility to outsiders, and their obsession with restoration all orbit.

**Seranthi** — *Stars & Fate.* The cosmic order, prophecy, and astronomical knowledge. Seers and stargazers serve Seranthi. The great observatory at Lethengarde was almost certainly built under her patronage.

**Varaneth** — *War & Honor.* Not a god of violence but of the conduct of war — discipline, valor, the line between a soldier and a butcher. Kynathi martial tradition is practiced under Varaneth's name. His temples are spartan; his worship is physical.

**Calidris** — *Craft & Making.* The divine patron of architecture, art, and the great works of civilization. The ruins of pre-Spellbreak Kynathi construction are, by those who still venerate Calidris, considered his greatest monument. His new works are not.

**Althindra** — *Memory & Wisdom.* The keeper of what has been known and what should be known. Her worship extends beyond the Kynathi — scholars and historians across Merata honor her in some form, though not always by that name. The great library at Tindaire is sometimes described as her earthly mirror.

**Syluneth** — *Nature & Life.* The living world and its long rhythms. For a long-lived people, the slow cycles of nature have different weight than they do for humans. Syluneth is patient, cyclical, and indifferent to individual fate.

**Morindel** — *Return & Passage.* The guide for those passing from the mortal world back to the divine realm. Kynathi theology does not frame death as ending but as return — a soul's journey home. Morindel oversees the threshold. He is venerated quietly and without fear. The Kynathi's deepest dread is not death but spiritual severance: being cut off from the return entirely — unable to reach the divine after the Spellbreak broke the path. This is what Kynathi believe Daeva worship ultimately risks.

**Luneth** — *Moon & Hidden Mystery.* The things that cannot be examined directly — knowledge at the edge of perception, the unseen. Luneth's worshippers tend to be mystics and those who deal in hidden things. Her domain overlaps most with what the Daeva offer, which is why some Kynathi regard her uneasily. The Anchorite heresy arose among her mystics and Morindel's, and orthodoxy has never let her cult forget it.

**Aeredyn** — *Time & Legacy.* The long view, the slow turning of ages, the persistence of what matters. Monuments, traditions, the act of remembering: these are Aeredyn's domain. For a people watching their civilization decline across centuries, Aeredyn holds a particular, melancholy weight.

## Locations

**Merata** — The large central continent and primary setting of Tessera. Merata bears the scars of the Spellbreak in visible and invisible ways: rifts, dead zones, places where magic behaves strangely, and ruins from what was clearly once something greater.

**Arlengh** — The northern continent, sealed since the Spellbreak behind permanent storm and ice. Before the catastrophe it was inhabited — by the human peoples whose descendants settled Isenfall generations before the crossing closed, and by the giants whose orphaned hulks still circle the world's oceans. What Arlengh is now, no one knows: whether its peoples warred and annihilated one another, whether the ice simply took them, or whether something survives behind the storms. No expedition has returned in living memory, and most never set out. Isenfall keeps the homeland as myth; the Sea Giants, presumably, keep it as something else.

**Isenfall** — The northern tundra and taiga region of Merata. Its people are culturally distinct from southern humans, descended from Arlengh settlers who crossed generations before the Spellbreak — a span that has itself become myth, since the catastrophe sealed the crossing behind them. Indigenous tribes predating those settlers have largely dwindled.

**Teutania** — The northwestern highlands of Merata, between Isenfall and the main human heartland. A distinct highland culture — martial, insular, proud — that has maintained independence from the Merathine Empire more through terrain than politics. Teutania has never been conquered — not by the Hegemony, not by the empire, not by anyone since — and the fact is the spine of highland identity, out of all proportion to the region's strategic weight.

**Pritani Jungle** — A vast jungle in southeastern Merata, home to the Reshai. Dense, ancient, and largely unmapped by outside peoples.

**Sahridi Desert** — A large desert believed to have been formed by the Spellbreak. The region was something else before — what the buried Vahari ruins suggest was a sophisticated civilization. The Sehrati are its current inhabitants.

**Cassidor** — The Merathine imperial capital, situated at the fertile delta of the Maren river. The greatest human city Merata has known; now grand but hollowed, its palaces and institutions still standing while its population has declined and its political authority has eroded. The emperor still holds court here. Real power is contested between Lorindale and Seradon factions at the imperial court. Cassidor's wealth derives from its position at the Maren delta — even a diminished empire controls an enormous amount of trade simply by existing at the mouth of Merata's primary river system.

**Tindaire** — Home to a massive college and library; the nearest thing Merata has to a neutral institution — and neutrality, at Tindaire, is not a virtue but a product, manufactured daily at great expense. Founded under Merathine patronage during the imperial peak, the college outgrew its patron by making itself indispensable to everyone: every crown endows chairs, every people sends scholars, and every power on the continent has deposited things in Tindaire's vaults that it wants kept, and kept sealed — the college's discretion being the collateral that guarantees its independence. The library holds the largest collection of pre-Spellbreak material still extant, the Tindaire Recension of the Nerebine Codex, the anomaly records, and sealed deposits its librarians catalogue but may not open. Its faculties shelter what other powers exile — Kynathi Namers who cannot go home, riftborn scholars, Alnathi correspondents of the Vigil — which is both its glory and the standing list of reasons half the continent distrusts it. Its newest and most delicate undertaking is the **Testimony**: a project to record the memories of the last living witnesses of the world before, over the Dominion's furious objection — Natari holds that its elders' memories are not Tindaire's to take, and more than one dying witness has defected north to be remembered on their own terms. Tindaire is where the competing theories of the Spellbreak are most seriously debated; it is honest with no one, useful to everyone, and it has outlived one empire already.

**Lorindale** — A prosperous human kingdom in the fertile heartland south of the Maren (see Geopolitics).

**Seradon** — A southern coastal kingdom on the warmer shores of Merata; the coast most exposed to Myrathi activity (see Geopolitics).

**Vrasar** — A northeastern human kingdom, rugged in terrain and culture; effectively where the Merathine Empire's reach ends, and now the frontier facing the Banner-Sworn (see Geopolitics).

**The Vezhan Steppe** — The eastern grasslands beyond Vrasar's marches, running past the edge of any imperial map. Home to horse clans human and made alike, now united for the first time in memory under a single Khal as the Banner-Sworn (see Geopolitics). The steppe's own history, spirit-traditions, and the Khal's identity remain to be developed.

**Juna** — A northern kingdom between the Dyrathi mountains and the tundra of Isenfall; warden of Illuvinel's approaches (see Geopolitics).

**Natari** — A mostly-intact pre-Spellbreak city on a dramatic clifftop of the southern coast, primarily inhabited by Kynathi. Enforces strict edicts to maintain ancient Kynathi tradition and high culture. Hostile to non-elves; dismissive of other *-athi* peoples. Once a center of Kynathi maritime trade, its southern heritage has been diminished as the sea below has grown more dangerous with Myrathi expansion. Capital of the Natari Dominion and the living heart of what remains of Kynathi civilization — precious and suffocating in equal measure. Natari's **Edict of Unnaming** makes the speaking, writing, or possession of Daevic names a crime within its walls; copies of the Nerebine Codex are contraband of the first order, and are burned in a public rite of erasure conducted without commentary, since even denunciation is attention.

**Lethengarde** — A deserted city in the northeast of Merata, and the great cautionary tale of post-Spellbreak scholarship. Its observatory predates the catastrophe — built under Seranthi's patronage in the Hegemony's age, when its instruments read the stars for fate. After the Spellbreak, the scholars who inherited it — a human-led college with members of most peoples — turned the instruments from the sky to the wounds: rifts, leylines, the spaces between planes, and at last the Graveland itself. It was, in effect, the great non-elven attempt at the Grave-Sight, pursued by lens and mirror instead of trance, and its northeastern isolation let the work run unsupervised for generations. Roughly two centuries ago, it stopped. The city did not die; it *emptied* — the only account is a supply-carter's, who arrived to open doors, set tables, and no one at all. No bodies were ever found. Whatever happened is called the Lethengarde Incident, and the correspondence the observatory had been sending to Tindaire — the **Lethengarde Letters**, increasingly strange in their final years — simply cuts off mid-series. Strange lights are still reported over the observatory, which may mean the instruments are still running; lingering near the city is said to produce madness, and every power that has quietly attempted a survey has quietly stopped.

**The Shattered Isles** — The center of Myrathi civilization, an archipelago in the Orathian Sea. The name suggests some catastrophe in their past, the details of which are not shared with outsiders. Among the few things outsiders claim to know of Myrathi tradition is that the sea began to speak at the Shattering, and that the Reading is the covenant that has kept the Myrathi alive since — though this, like everything about the Isles, arrives third-hand. What can be said with some confidence is that the Shattering predates the Spellbreak: whatever broke the Isles was not the breaking of the world, and the Reading is older than the silence of the gods.

**The Orathian Sea** — The body of water home to the Myrathi and, occasionally, to Sea Giants. Increasingly dangerous for other seafarers.

**Nerebyasca** — The Alnathi necropolis on the Blightlands' stable edge — and *necropolis* is meant precisely: a city whose citizens are dead. The living of Nerebyasca — **the Vigil**, its lay servitors, and the pilgrims and petitioners who come and go — are custodians and a minority; the population that matters is interred in the terraced vaults descending from the surface toward the boundary the dead must cross. In the oldest stratum of Alnathi doctrine the necropolis is a **harbor**: souls bound to the Graveland in life are tended here at the boundary, kept, the old teaching says, from dissolving with a world that may not last. The Vigil does not preach this. Its practice implies it daily.

The tending is not metaphor but labor. Because identity is the substance of persistence, the Vigil maintains its dead the way other cities maintain walls: the **Reading of the Rolls** runs day and night in rotating vigil, the names of the harbored dead spoken aloud in perpetual sequence — remembrance administered as life support, for a name that falls from the Rolls is a soul the harbor loses. The practice is the memory economy operated deliberately, in both directions — the same mechanism that feeds Daeva, turned to keeping the dead intact — and the Vigil's mastery of it is the reason the order, in the person of **Ashvenn the Twice-Grieved**, could produce the **Nerebine Codex**, whose uncopyable Vigil Original remains here, consulted only under rite. Whether the Vigil are the Codex's librarians, its operators, or its jailers depends on who is asked. Whether the harbored dead can be *reached* — spoken with, consulted — is a question outsiders ask constantly and the Vigil answers never; Alnathi funerary custom implies an answer, and implies it quietly.

Nerebyasca admits outsiders, which surprises those who finally see it. Petitioners come from every people: scholars seeking the Vigil's correspondence, the grieving seeking rites, and — steadily, discreetly — the dying, come to ask a berth in the harbor for souls their own traditions consign to silence. The Vigil refuses almost all of them. *Almost* is the word the desperate cross a continent for.

**The Blightlands** — A region of Merata's interior permanently damaged by the Spellbreak. Unstable magic, rifts, and Aberrations are more common here than anywhere else on the continent. Most peoples avoid the Blightlands entirely. The Blightlands were Alnathi country before they were the Blightlands — the continental interior settled by the Anchorite exiles millennia before the catastrophe — and the Alnathi homeland sits on the region's relatively stable edge. Their tolerance for its instability is partly long practice and partly constitutional: paid for, in the oldest accounting, at the Spellbreak itself.

**Gloamwood** — A strange forest bordering a Spellbreak-affected region of Merata. Its character is altered in ways that defy easy explanation: light behaves differently within it, the fauna are unusual, and travelers report disorientation and a persistent sense of being watched. Were-creatures are associated with Gloamwood, as are Aberrations that wander from the adjacent damaged zone. Alnathi tradition connects the forest's were-creatures to Syrath's touch upon Tessera — a lineage of completed transformations breeding true; the Nerebine Codex raises the question and pointedly declines to settle it. Gloamwood is not considered safe, and it is not mapped in any detail.

**Illuvinel** — A pre-Spellbreak ruin in the north of Merata, accessible from the regions around Isenfall and Juna. Once one of the most magnificent cities of the pre-Spellbreak world: elegant towers of marble and glass, sky bridges connecting elevated districts, grand tiered plazas, magical lifts, cascading waterfalls built into the architecture, and hanging gardens terraced across entire hillsides. All of it is collapsed and overgrown now. The towers have fallen, the magical lifts are inert, the waterfalls long dry. What remains is haunting in its beauty — shattered glass still catches the light, marble facades still bear carved reliefs, and occasionally a magical system reactivates without warning. Access to the ruin runs through Juna, whose crown licenses expeditions and taxes recoveries — a wardenship the Natari Dominion regards as the sale of desecration (see Geopolitics).

**Arhebel** — A pre-Spellbreak ruin in the south of Merata, primarily underground. Where Illuvinel is a collapsed surface city, Arhebel is a network of ancient vaults and catacombs extending deep below ground. The surface presence is minimal — worn stone entrances, a few collapsed structures — but the depths are extensive and largely unexplored. Pre-Spellbreak technology occasionally still functions within Arhebel, and artifacts regularly surface in the black markets of Lorindale and Cassidor. The lower levels have not been mapped. What Arhebel *was* is contested: the recovered artifacts suggest a treasury, the architecture suggests an archive, and the doors — which are built to seal from the outside — suggest a prison. The uncomfortable finding, reported independently by several expeditions and pursued by none, is that the masonry changes below a certain depth: the deeper levels are older than the upper ones, and past a certain point the stonework stops being Kynathi.

**Isrannaes** — A ruin carved directly into a mountainside — corridors and chambers cut from and reinforced within living rock, and built *late*: the stonework dates to the Waning, and it was made in a hurry, by a civilization that did not hurry. Abandoned since the Spellbreak. Its location makes it physically difficult to reach; the deep interior chambers are sealed, whether by the catastrophe or by design, and many have never been entered in the centuries since. What the Hegemony was building in its frightened final age — a refuge, an instrument, a vault, or something else — is unknown, and whether Isrannaes was meant to survive the Spellbreak or to prevent it is the kind of question Tindaire assigns to scholars it wants to lose.

**Loneridge** — A frontier settlement built around a contained Spellbreak rift; originally a mining town, later a ghost town, now the unlikeliest boomtown in Merata. Some sixty years ago a rift opened near the settlement and began consuming the surrounding landscape; a mixed-race party of five contained it (see *The Rift-Breakers of Loneridge*), and the containment altered the land, revealing access to previously undiscovered pre-Spellbreak ruins beneath. The rift remains — smaller, guarded, partially understood, still occasionally producing incursions — and the ruins keep yielding new chambers.

Everything else follows from those two facts. The Merathine licensing office technically requires documentation for any artifact removed from the ruins; it is four clerks, one soldier, and a stamp — yet the stamp still matters, because imperial paper still means something in Cassidor courtrooms, and the difference between licensed and unlicensed "Loneridge goods" is the difference between an inheritance and a hanging offense in three kingdoms. The black market thrives accordingly, in Cassidor, Seradon, and beyond. Loneridge also holds the largest riftborn community in Merata — the rift-touched gravitate to the one town where they are unremarkable — alongside imperial garrison soldiers, adventuring companies, priests of several traditions, smugglers, Vigil correspondents, criminal organizations, and scholars, all sharing the same taverns and competing for the same chambers. The names of the five founders are carved into the stone arch above the central well. Of the five, the three short-lived members are decades dead; the two elven members, Varenthil and Dhorash, may yet live. Neither has been seen in years, and Loneridge prefers not to know.

## Myths & Legends

### The Rift-Breakers of Loneridge

Five adventurers of different peoples — for reasons history has made murky — approached the Loneridge rift when it was open and actively consuming the surrounding landscape. Their names are carved above Loneridge's central well: **Caldren** (Human, Teutanian), **Varenthil** (Kynathi), **Dhorash** (Alnathi), **Kovrul** (Dyrathi), **Vikanu** (Reshai).

What the accounts agree on: Varenthil designed a magical containment that required something from each member of the party to complete. Kovrul built the physical anchor structure that still stands, reinforced over the years by Loneridge's residents. Caldren held the perimeter while the work was done. Vikanu found the passage into the rift's interior that no one else had located. What Dhorash did in the final moments is described differently in every version — in some accounts it was an act of communication with whatever was on the other side; in others it was sacrifice; in others it was simply the right thing said at the right moment by someone who understood what they were dealing with.

The rift did not close. The adventurers apparently did not intend it to. Caldren's reported response, when asked why they hadn't sealed it: *"You don't seal a door you might need to use."* Whether this was wisdom or hubris is still debated in Loneridge's taverns, usually by people who have never gone near the rift themselves.

## Magic

Before the Spellbreak, magic was a structured discipline — part of the underlying framework through which the Creators shaped and sustained Tessera. Practitioners worked within a reliable system, and the Kynathi in particular had developed it to extraordinary sophistication.

After the Spellbreak, the framework cracked. Magic still functions — the fragments of the old design still hold — but it is less predictable, less consistent, and occasionally wrong. Most magical practice in the current age is safe and functional. Wild magic events — spontaneous, uncontrolled magical discharge — are rare and tend to cluster near rifts, leyline fractures, or sites of significant Spellbreak damage. Places like Lethengarde, where the boundary between planes was deliberately and repeatedly probed, accumulate magical instability over time.

Different peoples have different relationships with post-Spellbreak magic. The Kynathi practice a refined tradition that attempts to reconstruct pre-Spellbreak methods, with uneven success. The Alnathi have developed approaches better suited to the current, messier reality, partly through their relationship with the Daeva. Human magical traditions vary enormously by region and culture. The Reshai practice of spirit veneration and ritual likely accesses something older and less dependent on the framework the Spellbreak damaged.

One thread runs beneath every people's practice: blood. In Tessera, blood is the living medium of the weave — the current through which a body carries its participation in creation. This is why blood oaths genuinely bind, why bloodline claims carry more than ceremony, why vampires are what they are, and why the practices mortals call blood magic are less a school than a temptation that every tradition polices differently.
