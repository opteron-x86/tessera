# Tessera - Geopolitics of Merata

*Companion to [world.md](world.md), [timeline.md](timeline.md), and [locations.md](locations.md). This file tracks the political order of Merata in **760 AS**.*

---

## Framing

Meratan politics in 760 AS are shaped by three overlapping facts:

1. The Spellbreak is ancient history for humans, but still living memory for a few long-lived witnesses.
2. The Kynathi Hegemony is gone as a practical empire, but not gone as a legal, religious, architectural, or cultural fact.
3. The Merathine Empire is the strongest human power, but it has plateaued and overextended.

Most states do not fight over whether the old world ended. They fight over what the present age means:

- **Continuation:** The old order never legally ended; it merely contracted.
- **Inheritance:** Human powers now hold the burden and right of rule.
- **Liberation:** The fall of the Hegemony freed peoples who were always older than imperial law.
- **Containment:** The first duty of states is to hold back rifts, frontier threats, cults, Aberrations, and ruined places.
- **Restoration:** The world is broken because the right order was lost and must be recovered.
- **Survival:** History is less important than food, roads, walls, ships, winter stores, and taxes.

This file treats existing entries as working canon. Anything marked **Open** is deliberately available for revision.

## The Political Map in 760 AS

### The Merathine Empire

The Merathine Empire is the largest human power in Merata. It is centered on **Cassidor**, the imperial capital at the fertile delta of the **Maren River**.

The Empire is not in full collapse, but it is past the easy part of expansion. Its roads, tax offices, river ports, military colonies, and provincial governors bind much of the human heartland together, but the system strains at the edges. Cassidor can still command armies, fund colleges, dredge channels, suppress bandit kings, and dictate terms to weaker neighbors. It can no longer do all of those things everywhere at once.

**Current condition:** Plateaued and overextended.

**Imperial claim:** The Merathine Empire presents itself as the necessary human answer to the post-Spellbreak age: not a reborn Kynathi Hegemony, but the only power large enough to keep roads open, ruins contained, rivers defended, and rival kingdoms from destroying one another.

**Internal pressure:**

- River wealth concentrates power in Cassidor and the Maren elite.
- Frontier provinces resent taxes spent elsewhere.
- Old noble houses claim pre-imperial rights.
- Provincial governors increasingly act like hereditary rulers.
- Scholars, engineers, and sanctioned mages are expensive and politically protected.
- Rift containment, ruin patrols, and anti-cult work consume resources without producing visible glory.

**External pressure:**

- Vrasar needs support against the Banner-Sworn but resents imperial control.
- Seradon needs naval support but does not want Cassidor ruling its harbors.
- Lorindale is too wealthy to ignore and too useful to crush.
- Teutania is costly to invade and easier to bargain with.
- Natari is too symbolically dangerous to erase and too diminished to safely respect.

**Open:** Decide whether the Merathine ruler is an emperor by hereditary bloodline, military acclamation, priestly sanction, senatorial election, or some mixed settlement born from the early successor centuries.

### Cassidor

Cassidor sits where the Maren fans into its fertile delta. It is the Empire's capital, warehouse, court, naval yard, tax engine, and symbolic center.

Cassidor's power comes from grain, river tolls, imperial administration, and the ability to turn distant crises into paperwork. It is cosmopolitan by necessity: merchants, petitioners, scholars, soldiers, envoys, refugees, river pilots, sanctioned mages, and foreign hostages all pass through it.

**Political role:** Cassidor is less a single city than the machine that makes the Merathine Empire possible.

**Tension:** The farther a province lies from the Maren system, the more Cassidor feels like an extractor rather than a protector.

### The Maren River

The **Maren River** is the primary river system of Merata: a branching network draining much of the continent's interior and fanning into the delta at Cassidor.

The Maren is a military road, trade artery, tax route, pilgrimage path, smuggling network, and political argument. Whoever controls its crossings and ports can feed armies, price grain, move officials, and decide which towns matter.

**Political role:** The Maren is the Empire's spine, but not all Maren powers are obedient to Cassidor.

**Common conflicts:**

- Toll rights between crown, city, temple, and noble estate.
- River piracy and sanctioned privateering.
- Disputes over dredging, floodworks, mills, and irrigation.
- Smuggling of ruin goods, forbidden relics, unsanctioned texts, and fugitive mages.

## Major Powers and Regions

### The Natari Dominion

**Current working version:** The Natari Dominion is a diminished Kynathi state centered on **Natari**, a mostly intact pre-Spellbreak city on the southern coast. It claims direct continuity with the old Kynathi Hegemony in law, rite, memory, and self-understanding.

The Dominion holds Natari and a contracted crescent of coastal and hinterland territory. It governs under old edicts, inherited offices, ritual precedence, and legal forms that most outsiders consider obsolete but not meaningless.

The Dominion has survived centuries of being written off because it still possesses things other powers cannot easily replace: pre-Spellbreak infrastructure, Kynathi magical traditions, ancient records, ritual authority among conservative Kynathi, and the prestige of endurance.

**Current condition:** Diminished, encircled, proud, and politically dangerous beyond its military size.

**Dominion claim:** The Hegemony did not end. Its rightful scope was broken by catastrophe and rebellion, but its law remains the highest law of Merata.

**What others fear:** Not conquest by Natari armies, but the possibility that Natari still holds old instruments, old claims, old names, or old witnesses that could delegitimize the successor order.

**Internal pressure:**

- Long-lived elders remember incompatible versions of the past.
- Younger Kynathi inherit imperial pride without imperial reach.
- Demographic weakness makes every death political.
- Some houses want accommodation with human powers; others call that surrender.
- The old edicts may require actions the Dominion can no longer enforce.

**External pressure:**

- Cassidor wants Natari contained, studied, and diplomatically managed.
- Human kingdoms use Natari as both warning and justification.
- Alnathi memory treats Natari's claims as morally compromised.
- Daeva cults and anti-Kynathi movements both find symbolic use in Natari's survival.

**Open:** Natari may be revised heavily. See **Natari Development Questions** below.

### Lorindale

Lorindale sits in the fertile heartland south of the Maren. It is prosperous, settled, agrarian, and politically valuable because food is power in a post-Spellbreak world.

Lorindale is not simply a breadbasket. Its wealth gives it patronage networks, marriage leverage, temple influence, militia depth, and the ability to fund scholars, fortifications, and mercenary companies without appearing imperial.

**Current working placement:** Lorindale is a human kingdom in the Merathine sphere, not a fully absorbed province. It is close enough to Cassidor to be pressured, but useful enough that the Empire prefers influence over annexation.

**Political claim:** Lorindale presents itself as the steward of ordinary continuity: harvest, law, family, contract, roads, and peace.

**Tension:** Lorindale benefits from imperial order while resenting imperial appetite.

**Open:** Decide whether Lorindale is an allied kingdom, a semi-autonomous imperial client, a dynastic partner, or a quiet rival preparing for imperial weakness.

### Seradon

Seradon is a southern coastal kingdom along the Orathian Sea. It is the Meratan coast most exposed to Myrathi activity, sea trade, storms, raiding, and the generational disasters of Sea Giant hulk landfalls.

Seradon looks outward more than inland powers do. Its politics are shaped by shipyards, harbor law, pilots, merchant houses, coastal fortresses, navigators, tide-readers, and arguments over whether the Myrathi are enemies, omens, trading partners, or something that human law cannot classify.

**Political claim:** Seradon claims that Cassidor does not understand the sea and that inland law fails at the waterline.

**Tension:** Seradon needs imperial grain, soldiers, and legitimacy, but fears imperial control of its ports.

**Common conflicts:**

- Whether to negotiate with Myrathi captains.
- Who pays for coastal defenses.
- Whether hulk warnings are superstition, Myrathi manipulation, or legitimate intelligence.
- Harbor rights for foreign ships and suspect cargo.

### Vrasar

Vrasar is a rugged northeastern kingdom where Merathine reach gives way to frontier pressure. Its marches face the **Vezhan Steppe**, where horse clans have united under a single Khal as the **Banner-Sworn**.

Vrasar is hardened by geography and threat. It values fortresses, riders, scouts, winter stores, signal towers, border law, and men and women who can make decisions before Cassidor sends permission.

**Political claim:** Vrasar claims to bleed so the heartland can argue.

**Tension:** Vrasar needs imperial money and troops, but imperial oversight can be fatal on a fast frontier.

**Current flashpoint:** The Banner-Sworn are no longer just raiders or rival clans. Their unification under one Khal turns the steppe into a strategic threat.

**Open:** Decide whether Vrasar is an imperial province, a client kingdom, a military ally, or a semi-independent march whose loyalty depends on continued support.

### The Vezhan Steppe and the Banner-Sworn

The Vezhan Steppe lies beyond Vrasar's marches. Its horse clans have united under a single Khal as the **Banner-Sworn**.

To settled powers, the Banner-Sworn are an invasion waiting to happen. To themselves, they may be a covenant, a liberation, a prophetic warband, a practical confederation, or the first steppe order strong enough to force the river kingdoms to listen.

**Political claim:** Open. The Banner-Sworn need a reason for unity stronger than plunder.

**Useful possibilities:**

- A charismatic Khal with an unprecedented oath system.
- A reaction to Merathine border forts and trade restrictions.
- A steppe religious movement built around sky, ancestor, or post-Spellbreak signs.
- A migration pressure from farther east.
- A claim that the old Hegemony or the Spellbreak stole lands, herds, or sacred routes.

### Juna

Juna is a northern kingdom wedged between the Dyrathi mountains and the Isenfall tundra.

Juna matters because it sits between worlds: human lowlands, Dyrathi holds, northern migrants, older indigenous fragments, and roads that become dangerous in winter. It is likely poorer than Lorindale and less prestigious than Cassidor, but its position makes it diplomatically durable.

**Political claim:** Juna presents itself as a keeper of passes, treaties, winter roads, and northern peace.

**Tension:** Juna survives by balancing stronger neighbors and peoples who do not share the same memory of land.

**Open:** Decide whether Juna's relationship with the Dyrathi is trade-based, treaty-bound, hostile, ritualized, or dependent on one major pass or hold.

### Teutania

Teutania is a northwestern highland region between Isenfall and the human heartland. It is martial, insular, and effectively independent because the terrain is hard to conquer.

Teutania's independence may not come from wealth or numbers, but from cost. Invading it would consume armies, winter stores, engineers, and political attention that Cassidor needs elsewhere.

**Political claim:** Teutania claims older rights of clan, oath, height, and local law against lowland centralization.

**Tension:** Teutania may hire out warriors, control passes, shelter fugitives, raid borders, or serve as a buffer depending on the season and the ruler.

### Isenfall

Isenfall is the northern tundra and taiga of Merata. Its settled peoples descend from Arlengh migrants who crossed before the Spellbreak sealed the north. Older indigenous tribes survive only in diminished fragments.

Isenfall is politically important because it preserves the wound of Arlengh. It is not just remote; it is the southern remainder of a lost northern world.

**Political claim:** Isenfall peoples may claim descent, exile, survival, or custodianship of memories no southern court can verify.

**Tension:** The question of who belongs in Isenfall is unresolved: Arlengh-descended settlers, older indigenous fragments, Juna's northern interests, Teutanian highland ties, and whatever may still lie beyond the storms.

## Non-Human and Extra-State Powers

### The Dyrathi Holds

The Dyrathi holds survived the surface catastrophe better than most civilizations. Some trade with surface powers; others remain closed and hostile.

The holds are not merely underground cities. They are alternate centers of continuity, craft, memory, and law. Surface states want Dyrathi metal, engineering, credit, and sealed knowledge, but the Dyrathi can often choose distance.

**Political role:** The Dyrathi are not in obvious decline, which makes them quietly unsettling to peoples who define the age as one of loss.

**Open:** Decide whether the Dyrathi act collectively in foreign policy or whether each hold is its own polity.

### The Myrathi of the Shattered Isles

The Myrathi occupy the Shattered Isles. Their fast warships are feared across the Orathian Sea because outsiders cannot predict them.

Myrathi action is governed by **the Reading**: a tidecaller reads sea, sky, weather, and encounter, then acts on that verdict. To outsiders, this makes Myrathi politics look irrational. To the Myrathi, outsider treaties may be less binding than the sea's verdict in the moment.

**Political role:** The Myrathi are a maritime power that cannot be handled by normal diplomacy alone.

**Tension:** Seradon and Cassidor want predictable rules. The Myrathi may not believe predictability is virtuous.

### Neresce and the Alnathi

Neresce is the Alnathi necropolis. Its true citizens are the dead kept in terraced vaults; the living Vigil, lay servitors, pilgrims, and petitioners are custodians.

Neresce is not a conventional state, but it has political gravity. It preserves names, deaths, records, rites, and perhaps versions of history that living states prefer buried.

**Political role:** Neresce is a memory power.

**Tension:** Any state that needs a lineage confirmed, a death witnessed, a forbidden history suppressed, or an oath interpreted may find itself dealing with the Vigil.

### The Sehrati and the Sahridi Desert

The Sehrati inhabit the Sahridi Desert and treat the buried Vahari ruins as sacred ground.

To outside powers, the Sahridi Desert is a barrier, a ruin-field, and a temptation. To the Sehrati, it is not empty land. It is a sacred aftermath.

**Political role:** The Sehrati complicate every outside attempt to excavate, exploit, map, or claim the Vahari ruins.

**Open:** Decide whether any major power formally recognizes Sehrati sovereignty, avoids the desert by taboo, violates it secretly, or treats them as guardians of something too dangerous to disturb.

### The Reshai and the Pritani Jungle

The Reshai are indigenous to the Pritani Jungle. Outsiders know little because the jungle is dense, ancient, and largely unmapped by them.

The Reshai may not fit Meratan state categories. Their politics may be kinship-based, mask-based, spirit-mediated, martial, ritual, or intentionally opaque to outsiders.

**Political role:** The Pritani Jungle is one of the places where imperial maps fail.

**Open:** Decide whether Cassidor, Seradon, or another power is currently pushing into the Pritani Jungle for timber, relics, trade, or strategic routes.

## Present Flashpoints

These are useful pressure points for 760 AS.

1. **The Banner-Sworn Question:** Vrasar wants immediate support. Cassidor wants intelligence, leverage, and time. The Banner-Sworn may already be more unified than anyone admits.
2. **The Natari Question:** Is the Dominion a relic, a lawful continuity, a future threat, or a necessary keeper of knowledge?
3. **The Maren Burden:** Floodworks, tolls, dredging, grain prices, and river piracy can become imperial crises faster than frontier wars.
4. **The Seradon Coast:** Myrathi encounters and hulk omens make normal maritime policy unreliable.
5. **The Ruin Economy:** Pre-Spellbreak artifacts, sealed chambers, and dangerous scholarship create wealth and corruption.
6. **The Rift Frontier:** Places like Loneridge prove that contained rifts can become boomtowns, which encourages reckless imitation.
7. **The Memory War:** Kynathi witnesses, Alnathi records, Dyrathi archives, vampire testimony, and human court histories do not agree.

## Natari Development Questions

Natari is the most important soft-canon pressure point in the political map. The current version works, but several alternatives may produce different kinds of story.

### 1. Was Natari the old capital of the Hegemony?

**Option A: Yes, Natari was the imperial capital.**

This makes Natari's claim clean and potent. The old throne still stands, even if its empire does not.

**Best if:** The setting wants Natari to feel like Rome, Byzantium, or an ancient holy-imperial capital whose diminished court still terrifies diplomats.

**Risk:** Too much legitimacy may make it hard to explain why Natari has not become the center of every political dispute.

**Option B: No, Natari was the surviving legal refuge.**

The actual capital was destroyed, lost, shifted into an anomalous state, or became inaccessible. Natari survived with enough offices, archives, witnesses, and rites to claim continuation.

**Best if:** The setting wants ambiguity, disputed law, and a Dominion whose legitimacy is powerful but attackable.

**Risk:** Requires explaining why Natari's continuity claim persuaded enough Kynathi to endure for 760 years.

**Option C: Natari was a sacred or juridical capital, not the administrative capital.**

The Hegemony may have had separate centers for law, ritual, military command, scholarship, and imperial residence.

**Best if:** The old Hegemony should feel sophisticated and strange rather than modeled on a single capital-state.

**Risk:** More complex, but probably richer.

**Recommendation:** Option C, or Option B with elements of C. Natari should not merely be "the old capital." It is more interesting if its claim rests on rites, law, archives, and surviving offices, while the administrative heart of the Hegemony was destroyed, lost, or transformed by the Spellbreak.

### 2. What does the Natari Dominion actually want?

**Option A: Restoration.**

Natari wants its empire back.

**Pros:** Clear motivation.

**Cons:** Too simple unless the Dominion has no realistic path to achieve it.

**Option B: Continuity.**

Natari wants recognition that its law never ended, even if practical control is impossible.

**Pros:** More subtle. This makes diplomacy, treaties, titles, marriages, ruins, and old rights matter.

**Cons:** Less immediately dramatic.

**Option C: Preservation.**

Natari knows restoration is impossible and is trying to preserve Kynathi life, memory, magic, and records through decline.

**Pros:** Tragic and politically flexible.

**Cons:** Could make Natari too passive unless internal factions disagree.

**Recommendation:** Use all three as factions. Publicly, Natari claims continuity. Reactionaries dream of restoration. Pragmatists pursue preservation.

### 3. Why has no one conquered Natari?

Possible answers:

- Its old defenses still work unpredictably.
- Conquest would unite Kynathi factions that currently hate one another.
- Cassidor prefers a contained Natari to a martyr Natari.
- Natari has records, oaths, hostages, or ruin-keys that make invasion dangerous.
- The Dominion is protected by treaty, taboo, geography, or mutual fear.
- The city itself may not be safe for occupiers.

**Recommendation:** Combine political deterrence with old infrastructure. Natari should be conquerable in theory but not worth the risk unless a ruler is desperate, fanatical, or badly misinformed.

### 4. How do humans talk about Natari?

Useful competing human views:

- **Imperial Cassidor:** Natari is a managed relic and a source of dangerous legitimacy.
- **Lorindale:** Natari is a warning against pride and a useful counterweight to Cassidor.
- **Seradon:** Natari is a southern neighbor whose coast, ships, and archives cannot be ignored.
- **Vrasar:** Natari is old history; the real threat rides from the steppe.
- **Popular human memory:** Natari is where old elves still write laws for a world that escaped them.

### 5. What should remain unresolved?

Strong mysteries to preserve:

- Whether Natari's highest legal claim is technically correct.
- Whether it still has direct testimony about the Spellbreak.
- Whether its oldest surviving edicts are complete, altered, or impossible to obey.
- Whether its continuity protects the world in some unseen way.
- Whether destroying Natari would solve a problem or release one.
