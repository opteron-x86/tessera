# Tessera — Geopolitics of Merata

*A companion document to the world reference. This file holds the Merathine successor landscape, Natari's position, imperial decline, and the Banner-Sworn pressure formerly summarized in `world.md`.*

## Geopolitics

What follows charts the Merathine sphere — the imperial successor landscape of human Merata — together with the powers that press upon it. It is a sampling, not a census: the continent holds more polities, peoples, and arrangements than any single account, and the map here is explicitly incomplete. The internal politics of the non-human powers — the Dominion's factions, the holds' councils, the Isles, the steppe — await their own treatments.

### The Natari Dominion

The Kynathi rump state — the direct continuation, in law and in self-understanding, of the pre-Spellbreak Kynathi Hegemony that once covered much of Merata. The Dominion holds Natari and a contracted crescent of coastal and hinterland territory, governs it under the old edicts, and has spent seven centuries in the condition of an empire that refuses to finish falling: diminished, encircled by successor peoples, periodically written off, and still standing. It is past its capacity for conquest and nowhere near past its capacity for harm — Dominion arts, Dominion knowledge, and Dominion gold move through Meratan politics far beyond Dominion borders, and its diplomacy is patient, contemptuous, and effective. Human courts that deal with Natari come away feeling they have negotiated with something that regards the entire post-Spellbreak order as a squatters' arrangement it has not yet troubled to contest. The Dominion's true strategic problem is not any rival but arithmetic: the witnesses are dying, the birth-houses are quiet, and every policy Natari pursues is downstream of a question it never speaks aloud — what a restoration is *for*, if no one is left to restore.

### The Merathine Empire

Humanity's bid for the hegemony the Kynathi lost — made, in hindsight, about three centuries too early. Founded roughly a century after the Spellbreak, when the first Merathine united the delta city of Cassidor with the river kingdoms of the Maren under a single crown, the empire expanded through the disordered generations that followed until, at its peak, it governed more of Merata than any human polity before or since. The founding was framed then, and is taught still, as a *restoration* — not a new crown but the return of human kingship after the long clientage, with the first Merathine's blood-claim reaching back, by genealogies of contested honesty, to the kings the Hegemony had unmade. The claim's truth matters less than its work: it makes the empire the heir of something older than the Kynathi conquest, and human rule in Merata a resumption rather than an invention. Whether it named itself for the continent, or for its founder, or the continent for it, is a question Tindaire's antiquarians genuinely cannot settle; the crown has always preferred the ambiguity, which asserts universal rule either way.

It has been receding for some three hundred years. A formal imperial structure still exists — an emperor of the Merathine line still sits in Cassidor — but the empire now controls perhaps a third of what it once did. Its armies cannot enforce edicts far from the heartland; its legal institutions and cultural influence persist across successor kingdoms even where its military does not. The dynasty's blood-claim is not merely ceremonial in Meratan eyes: in a world where blood carries the current of participation and identity, an unbroken founding line is believed to *hold* something, and rival court factions in Lorindale and Seradon back rival branches of the family accordingly. The empire survives partly through inertia, partly through ceremony, and partly because no single successor state has been strong enough to formally dissolve it. Cassidor itself is grand but hollowed — its palaces and institutions still stand, its population has declined, and it runs on Maren delta trade wealth and the accumulated prestige of its past. The founding itself — who the first Merathine was, and by what mandate the crown was claimed — is wrapped in the **Kingsblood**, the imperial cult of the restored royal line, treated with the rest of human religion in a companion document.

### The Maren River

The primary river system of Merata — a branching network draining much of the continent's interior, fanning into a fertile delta at Cassidor. It is simultaneously a highway and a border, a source of agricultural wealth and political contestation. The Merathine Empire was built around control of the Maren and its tributaries; the empire's fading has made the river's management a persistent source of inter-kingdom tension.

### The Successor Landscape

The empire's recession left behind not four neat kingdoms but a heterogeneous landscape — successor crowns, chartered leagues, march-lords, free cities, and peripheries that were never anyone's subjects. The powers below are the largest pieces, not the whole board.

**Lorindale** sits in the fertile heartland south of the Maren — the granary that believes it is the heir. The most pro-imperial of the successor states, it treats the empire less as a sovereign than as an inheritance to be managed into its own hands: Lorindale gold endows imperial offices, Lorindale jurists staff the imperial courts, and Lorindale backs its favored branch of the Merathine line with a patience that assumes the succession is a matter of time. Its expansionist interests are the best funded in Merata, organized through chartered land companies whose paper promises estates in the Dyrathi approaches and the Gloamwood margins — charters that sell briskly in Cassidor whether or not anyone survives to claim the land. Lorindale's weakness is the mirror of its strength: everything it is depends on the river, on the imperial law that governs the river, and on the assumption that the game of inheritance will be decided in courtrooms rather than fields.

**Seradon** holds the southern coast — pragmatic, maritime, and quietly the most worldly of the kingdoms, because it is the one that lives with the sea and everything the sea contains. Its admiralty maintains the continent's only serious archive of Myrathi encounters and tolerates the coastal *quiet-water customs* — offerings, silences, hull-marks — that official doctrine dismisses as superstition and every Seradine captain observes anyway. Seradon is also the kingdom that actually deals with the Natari Dominion: Dominion trade moves through Seradine ports under agreements neither party publicizes, and Seradon's court has learned what Lorindale's has not — what it costs, and what it earns, to negotiate with something older than your civilization. Its conviction is that the coast can survive the empire's death, and its policy is to be positioned for the day.

The Lorindale–Seradon rivalry is not temperamental but historical: two generations ago the kingdoms fought the **Salt War** over the Maren mouths, and the **Treaty of the Mouths** that ended it — brokered by the crown, satisfying no one — divided the delta tolls by a formula each side regards as robbery. Every court intrigue between them since is that treaty, continued by other means.

**The Maren League** is not a kingdom at all: a chain of chartered merchant cities along the river's middle course, created by imperial charter in the empire's building age to move grain and man the locks, and now, in the fading, effectively self-governing. The League's freedom rests entirely on imperial paper — its charters are grants from the crown, valid precisely as long as a crown exists to have granted them — which makes the League the strangest faction in imperial politics: cities that despise the emperor and need the empire, paper loyalists who would bankroll the throne's defense and celebrate its humiliation in the same season. In any succession crisis the League will back whichever claimant confirms the charters; it says so openly, and calls it honesty.

**Vrasar** is the northeastern successor state — rugged, cold, martial, and now the continent's eastern shield. The Banner-Sworn's probing raids have put the kingdom on a war footing: the marches are fortifying, refugees drift west, and Vrasar hires made-peoples mercenaries to fight made-peoples riders, an irony the kingdom has stopped finding remarkable. Its relationship with the Dyrathi holds along its northern border has deepened into dependence — Dyrathi steel, Dyrathi credit, Dyrathi tolls waived against future considerations — and the holds now own more of Vrasar's war than either party advertises. Vrasar maintains formal imperial membership and open contempt for it; its calls for aid go unanswered, and the kingdom increasingly behaves like what it may soon declare itself to be. Whether the horde makes Vrasar the empire's grave or its refounding is the openest question in Meratan politics.

**Juna** is the smallest of the principal crowns and the strangest: a northern kingdom wedged between the Dyrathi mountains and the Isenfall tundra, whose real wealth is a dead city. Juna controls the approaches to Illuvinel, the most magnificent ruin of the Kynathi golden age, and has built its treasury on wardenship of the corpse — licensing expeditions, taxing recoveries, and hanging unlicensed diggers with a rigor the Merathine office at Loneridge can only envy. The trade has made Juna rich beyond its size and placed it in permanent, carefully managed conflict with the Natari Dominion, which regards Illuvinel as sacred bones and Juna's licenses as the sale of desecration. Juna's court has become unexpectedly expert in Dominion diplomacy, Isenfall custom, and the practical theology of what may and may not be safely removed from a dead elven city — knowledge it did not want and cannot now afford to lose.

Beyond and between the named powers lies the rest of the landscape: march-lords along every frontier whose crown allegiance is a courtesy and whose real policy is made with whoever lives across the border; free cities holding old immunities; Pactorate vowhouse estates, licensed shrines, and pilgrimage towns whose sacredness is inseparable from imperial paper; and the never-imperial peripheries — Teutania, Isenfall, and others unnamed — which were no one's successors because they were never anyone's subjects.

### The Political Divide

Meratan pamphleteers describe a continent divided between two parties: those who would push the human frontier outward into non-human lands, and those who would hold it and trade across it. The description is a simplification that everyone uses and everyone knows to be one. The political weather is real; the tidy division is the pamphleteers'.

What actually exists is a scatter of local, material conflicts over which the frame gets draped. The **Weir War**, fought on one of the Maren's eastern tributaries over water-rights and mill-weirs between a Lorindale march and a League city, killed several hundred people before anyone called it ideological. The **Greenmarch collapse** — a chartered land company whose paper promised estates in the Gloamwood margins and whose surveyors never returned — ruined investors in two kingdoms and made *charter man* a slur in both. The **Harrow Compact**, a border lord's private treaty of tolls and mutual defense with a Dyrathi hold, was formally disavowed by his crown and is quietly honored by it, because the alternative is a war the crown cannot fund. And the riot at **Vellin**, where a rift incursion was answered with a pogrom against the town's riftborn quarter, is the kind of event both tendencies claim as proof of their case. From incidents like these the pamphlet-frame is assembled; *charter men* and *treaty men* are what the tendencies call each other, and almost no one calls themselves. Non-human peoples, for their part, cannot simply ally with a friendly kingdom — they must navigate the actual scatter, court by court and march by march.

Over all of it sits the **imperial question**. The Merathine line runs thin — the emperor is old, the direct succession uncertain — and in a world where blood is believed to hold something, the inheritance of the founding blood is not academic. Lorindale and Seradon back rival branches; the Maren League backs the charters and says so; Vrasar asks why it bleeds for a crown that sends letters; and a quiet position whispered everywhere holds that the empire should be allowed to finish dying so that something real can be built from the estate. The Banner-Sworn have scrambled every calculation at once, because an external threat strengthens every argument — conquest, alliance, unity, dissolution — and nothing clarifies a continent's politics like a horde.

### The Vezhan Steppe and the Banner-Sworn

East of Vrasar's marches, beyond the last imperial milestone, the grasslands run to the horizon: the Vezhan Steppe, home for centuries to horse clans both human and made — orcish, goblin, and mixed-blood peoples who drifted east after their makers' fall and learned the steppe's economy of motion. The clans raided one another and the frontier in the ordinary way of steppes until, within living memory, they stopped raiding one another. A single Khal now commands what the clans call the **Banner-Sworn** — a confederation of unprecedented size, bound by oaths sworn in blood, which in Tessera is not a figure of speech. The Banner-Sworn have taken the eastern marches' measure in probing raids for a decade; Vrasar arms; Juna watches; the empire sends letters. What the Khal intends — conquest, tribute, a homeland, or something stranger — is unknown, and the rumor that something rides with the horde, whispering the hunt onward, is exactly the kind of rumor Merata's frontiers always produce and can never confirm. The Banner-Sworn are the one unambiguously rising power on a declining continent, and every court in Merata has begun to reckon with what that means.

## Geopolitics Design Notes

*Out-of-world reference: what is true, what is belief, and what should stay unresolved.*

**True:** The Natari Dominion is diminished but dangerous. The Merathine Empire still exists and has been receding for roughly three centuries. Cassidor remains the imperial capital. Lorindale, Seradon, the Maren League, Vrasar, Juna, Teutania, Isenfall, the Dyrathi holds, and the steppe all exert different kinds of pressure on the imperial order. The Banner-Sworn are the setting's clearest rising political force.

**Belief, not established reality:** The Merathine dynasty's founding blood-claim. Claims that any successor state is the rightful heir of the empire. Pamphlet descriptions of Meratan politics as a clean frontier-versus-trade divide. Rumors that something rides with the Banner-Sworn and whispers the hunt onward.

**Deliberately unresolved (recommend never confirming too early):** The Khal's ultimate intention. Whether the Banner-Sworn will break the empire, refound it, bypass it, or be turned by forces no court has named correctly. Whether the imperial guarantee truly binds seven centuries of oaths to the Merathine line. Whether the empire should be allowed to die.

**Open hooks:** A succession crisis in Cassidor. A Pactorate guarantee case that could break imperial law. Vrasar choosing between imperial membership and survival. A Lorindale charter disaster. Seradon choosing the sea over the court. Juna caught between relic wealth and Dominion outrage. Dyrathi credit as a hidden war front. The Banner-Sworn forcing every local political simplification to fail.

**Guardrails:** Keep politics local, material, and institutional. The pamphlet-frame is in-world simplification, not setting truth. The rising force must remain active enough that decline has a clock. Human politics should remain tied to blood, oath, paper, river, relics, and frontier violence rather than abstract ideology alone.
